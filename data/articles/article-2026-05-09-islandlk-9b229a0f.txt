Ravi K’s stark assessment of emerging picture

New Democratic Front (NDF) MP and former UNP Finance Minister and Minister of Power and Energy Ravi Karunanayake delivers a stark assessment of Sri Lanka’s economic and governance crisis, warning that the country remains financially fragile amid weak growth, mounting external debt pressures, and inadequate safeguards in an increasingly digital economy.

In this interview with the Sunday Island, he criticizes what he calls the government’s “system maintenance” approach under President Anura Kumara Dissanayake, highlights the absence of investment and pro-growth policies, and calls for sweeping reforms to the Central Bank. He also raises concerns over energy policy missteps, fiscal constraints, and the need for unified political leadership to drive sustainable economic recovery.

A: I believe our financial situation is precarious and highly vulnerable. This is largely because our systems have broken down during the transition to a digitalized world. In this shift from traditional approaches to digitization, we have failed to adequately safeguard the transformation against Cybercrime and other forms of technological intrusion. Sri Lanka is now in a position where we do not even know the true extent of the threat or when a serious cyber attack might occur.

On the political front, the government came to power promising a ‘system change’ and ambitious ideas for modernization. However, what we are witnessing is not a system change, but rather system maintenance. This so-called ‘system maintenance’ lacks the human capacity required to deliver meaningful results. At present, the entire political discourse appears to revolve around a single buzzword: AKD (Anura Kumara Dissanayake). In my view, this alone is insufficient to rescue an economy from bankruptcy or steer the country through the turbulent period we are facing.

A: Take our external debt as an example. While the Paris Club and bilateral partners have agreed to a debt moratorium, we still face deadlines such as the one in April 2028. We are not generating new dollar inflows; instead, we are relying on loans and domestic rupee liquidity, which does not resolve the external debt issue. There is no fresh capital expenditure, no new projects, and no sustainable investments entering the country.

Maintaining the status quo is not enough; we require growth of around 10% to 11% to double our capacity and meet debt obligations. Talking about 5% growth is inadequate when the goal is to transform the economy.

In addition, there are external shocks such as the situation in the Middle East. Every government faces external challenges; this is not unique to the present administration. What matters is the capacity, resources, and knowledge to manage such shocks when they arise.

Q: How would you describe the financial discipline of the current NPP administration?

A: It is interesting. Ranil Wickremesinghe established a very high benchmark for government revenue through heavy taxation, perhaps excessively so. Now, Anura Kumara Dissanayake is essentially maintaining that same framework from the 2023–2025 period. While I appreciate their adherence to fiscal discipline, revenue collection alone is not sufficient.

Under the Public Fiscal Responsibility Act, expenditure is capped at 13% of GDP. If GDP growth stagnates and currency depreciation occurs, we effectively spend less than in the previous year. What we need is increased spending to stimulate growth, as expenditure drives economic expansion. That element is currently missing.

Q: You have been vocal about the Central Bank. What is your main criticism of its financial management?

A: I consider the Central Bank to be the biggest obstacle to the country’s growth. This is not a personal criticism of the Governor, but rather a concern about institutional governance. I have previously told Ranil Wickremesinghe that the Central Bank should not have been granted the level of independence it now enjoys. Prior to independence, it contributed to the country’s bankruptcy; now, it risks undermining the banking system itself.

Recently, when officials appeared before the Committee on Public Finance, their professionalism was severely lacking. They were unable to adequately explain how a bank had misled them for a period of three years.

Q: What specific reforms are you proposing for the Central Bank?

A: I have proposed the establishment of a Parliamentary Select Committee to ensure that the Central Bank’s mandate shifts from a largely “laissez-faire” approach to one focused on growth, employment generation, and reserve accumulation. The Bank should operate in close coordination with the Ministry of Finance rather than in isolation.

We cannot allow unelected officials to dictate monetary policy while expecting elected representatives to remain silent. A country cannot be developed solely through anti-inflationary, contractionary policies. What we need is a supply-side, pro-growth strategy similar to what we observe in India under Prime Minister Modi.

Q: Speaking of India, how do you interpret their recent election results in relation to Sri Lanka’s future?

A: Voters across the world are increasingly cynical. In Sri Lanka, the public is in a state of uncertainty; they do not fully understand the complexities of governance, which has become far more challenging than many realize. This is a period in which proven competence must take precedence over optimistic narratives.

We must reflect on our own history such as the economic liberalization of 1977 and consider a renewed vision for the future. People often believe that opportunities are better elsewhere, only to recognize the reality once they reach that point.

Q: What is your assessment of the current situation in the power and energy sector, given rising prices and issues with procurement and substandard coal?

A: Governance challenges are not unique to any one administration; every government encounters new problems over time. However, the current crisis stems from policy misdirection. Previously, renewable energy supported by Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) was considered the most viable way to reduce foreign exchange pressure and utilize domestic resources.

Unfortunately, the government adopted an anti-renewable energy stance, resulting in dependence on substandard coal and costly thermal power generation. This has led to an estimated financial impact of 110 to 120 billion rupees. This burden must either be absorbed by the government which lacks sufficient fiscal space, given that it amounts to roughly 0.5% of GDP or passed on to consumers.

Q: Regarding the political landscape, what are your views on discussions about the UNP and SJB collaborating or merging into a single party?

A: Every major political party in this country has had the opportunity to govern and demonstrate its capabilities. My ‘revolutionary approach’ is that all parties should come together around a set of minimum national objectives. While this may appear impractical, the reality is that the maximum number of parties must unite to address the declining expectations of the public.

If growing frustration and radical sentiments are not addressed, the country risks descending into severe poverty and instability. What we need is transformative thinking that confronts issues directly, rather than relying on traditional, family-centered political approaches. India provides an example, where governance has taken precedence over rhetoric, enabling leaders such as Modi to secure electoral success across regions. We require individuals who deliver results, not merely those who speak.

Q: Have you shared this vision with senior political leaders, and how have they responded?

A: The reality is evident; those who fail to recognize these changes will eventually become irrelevant. I communicate these ideas in a professional manner, rather than acting like a ‘bull in a china shop.’ The central message is that the concerns of the people cannot be ignored; they must be acknowledged, addressed, and resolved.

A: I urge people to believe in what is achievable rather than what is unrealistic. This country has a long history of promises—rice at 25 cents, eight kilos of grains, cheap sugar, and bread at Rs 3.50—that ultimately erode economic foundations. We must learn from development models in countries such as Indonesia, Thailand, Malaysia, Singapore, and Vietnam, which have moved away from welfare-heavy approaches.

We must shift from distributing poverty to creating and sharing prosperity. While other nations have focused on growth, we have too often moved in the opposite direction. That is the fundamental change required. I also call upon the younger generation, those under 30, to adopt a more revolutionary and disciplined mindset. They must not allow impulsive thinking to guide their decisions. Instead, they should evaluate performance, rely on knowledge, and observe global trends to broaden their perspectives.

They must understand that we should work towards the future, not borrow against it. The question is not whether we can develop this country, but whether we truly want to. The future of Sri Lanka now lies in the hands of the younger generation. Merely holding a smartphone and believing that artificial intelligence places the world at their fingertips is not sufficient. That may be the AI of today, but it is not the AI that will define future generations.
