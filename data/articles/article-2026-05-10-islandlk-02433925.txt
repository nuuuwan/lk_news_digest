Leadership Under Fire – Lessons from Dankotuwa – Episode 4

LESSONS FROM MY CAREER: SYNTHESISING MANAGEMENT THEORY WITH PRACTICE – PART 36

My earlier episodes described my encounters with interfering politicians and labour challenges at Dankotuwa Porcelain. This chapter, however, deals with something more subtle—but equally demanding: the complexity of managing a large, sophisticated organisation under constant pressure and uncertainty.

When I re-joined Dankotuwa Porcelain PLC as a Director, both the Chairman and CEO were on the verge of leaving. I felt the board was not being properly informed. At board meetings, I began questioning even the data that was presented. That did not make me popular. In fact, I became something of a nuisance. The CEO left soon after.

During this period, I was attending a programme in Japan when the company was selecting a new CEO. The Japanese director insisted on meeting me over lunch, even though I had reserved that day for my last-minute shopping. He wanted my opinion on the shortlisted candidate.

After reviewing the profile, I felt strongly that the individual would be a misfit. Dankotuwa was not a simple operation. It was far more complex than a tile factory or a garment plant. With over 1,000 experienced workers, strong unions, and a demanding export market, the environment required not just competence, but resilience and maturity.

The board, however, took a different view and appointed him.

Three days later, the Chairman called me. The new CEO had walked into his house with his resignation letter. The job had overwhelmed him. I said nothing. There was no need to say, “I told you so.”

By now, with the resignation of the Chairman, I was once again made the non-executive Chairman.

We tried once again to recruit a new CEO. The next CEO was mature and steady, capable of controlling operations, but lacked energy. He lasted a year and then resigned, saying that I pushed him too hard. The next one was dynamic and intelligent, but lacked the human touch. He disrupted relationships, alienated staff, and even offended the Japanese directors. He too left.

Eventually, we operated with acting CEOs until the Japanese directors persuaded me to take over full-time as Chairman and CEO. I initially resisted, as I was still engaged in restructuring the Merchant Bank of Sri Lanka, which was my full-time job. I had given my word to the staff that I would not leave until their suspended benefits were restored. Once that promise was fulfilled and dividends were paid to shareholders after many years, I moved to Dankotuwa full-time.

Looking back, this episode reinforced a simple but critical truth: selecting the right leader is not about qualifications alone. It is about fit—fit with the organisation, its people, its culture, and its challenges. Many Boards underestimate this.

One of the major external shocks we faced was the withdrawal of the GSP+ concession. For exporters like us, this was a serious blow. GSP+ allowed reduced tariffs when exporting to the European Union. Without it, our products became less competitive overnight.

I took the trouble to understand the issue deeply, learning from experts in trade policy. It was clear that the concession had been granted on certain conditions, which the Government had failed to meet. Instead of engaging constructively with the European Union, political rhetoric took over.

At a meeting with the EU Ambassador, he made a striking remark: “An international cricket match must be played on a proper pitch—not on the road.” In other words, he maintained that this issue should be negotiated by the government with the European Union rather than making disparaging remarks about the scheme or making demands. The message was clear. International trade requires discipline and adherence to agreed-upon rules.

I led efforts to advocate for retaining GSP+, but was eventually asked by the Government to stop campaigning, as it was not official government policy and was seen as a threat to the country’s sovereignty. Meanwhile, our competitors—especially Bangladesh—gained a significant advantage under a GSP+ section, which allowed duty-free access for “anything but arms”. This facility was available to countries with lower development levels.

Yet, despite these setbacks, we managed to retain most of our customers. Relationships, built over years, proved more resilient than policy shocks.

Then came the 2008–2009 global financial crisis. I happened to be in the United States at the time and saw first-hand the panic following the collapse of Lehman Brothers.

The impact on our business was immediate and severe. Our order book, which typically covered nine months, shrank to barely half a month. Major customers cancelled orders. European buyers struggled to make payments as their banks tightened credit.

The nature of our product made matters worse. Porcelain is not an essential purchase. Unlike garments or vehicles, which must be replaced regularly, porcelain items last for generations. In times of crisis, they are the last thing customers buy.

The Government’s response did not help. An incentive scheme was introduced to reward exporters who increased exports. It was a well-intentioned but impractical idea. Demand had collapsed. No amount of persuasion could make buyers order what they could not sell.

Internally, the pressure mounted. Salaries were delayed. Payments to suppliers were stretched. Fortunately, our banks and suppliers—both local and foreign—showed understanding and gave us time.

However, not everyone appreciated the gravity of the situation. At a union meeting, a head office representative openly admitted he did not understand what a “global recession” was. At another meeting, a senior Ministry official questioned why we could not pay salaries on time, given that his own salary arrived on time.

It was difficult to remain patient. But such moments reminded me that communication is often as important as strategy. People resist what they do not understand.

We undertook an educational project to inform our floor-level employees about the current global situation and what other Asian factories were experiencing. The message sank in for some, but not for others who believed that labour must never co-operate with management.

In the midst of this crisis, a foreign bank suddenly informed us that it was withdrawing all our facilities. Their regional office had classified the ceramic industry as declining and not sustainable.

I was furious. I recalled a remark often made by the respected banker Edgar Gunatunga—that foreign banks give you an umbrella when the weather is fine and take it away when it rains.

I wrote a strong letter to the bank, quoting this very statement. The letter created quite a stir. Senior officials requested a meeting, and after detailed discussions, they restored our facilities. In a gesture of humour, they even presented me with an umbrella.

More importantly, I had already prepared contingency plans. Local bankers, including Mr Anil Amarasuriya, had assured support if needed. The key was to maintain credibility. We made it a point never to default on our obligations.

To reduce working capital, we introduced lean management practices and reduced raw material inventory. In theory, it was sound. In practice, it failed—at least initially.

Suppliers began defaulting. An Indian kaolin supplier stopped operations due to environmental restrictions. A Chinese supplier blamed unexpected weather conditions. Our carefully balanced clay mixtures were disrupted.

Porcelain production is highly sensitive. Even slight changes in raw material composition affect shrinkage, colour, and strength. Our laboratory team had to constantly experiment to maintain quality.

This was a difficult period. However, we eventually stabilised the process and made lean management work in a more adapted form. It taught me that management techniques cannot be applied blindly. They must be adapted to the context.

Two important lessons stand out from this phase of my career.

First, leadership is situational. The qualities required in a CEO vary depending on the organisation’s stage and challenges. A technically competent or highly intelligent leader may still fail if they lack emotional intelligence or contextual understanding. This aligns with what modern management calls contingency theory—there is no single best style of leadership.

Second, resilience is built through relationships and credibility. During the financial crisis, it was not policies or systems that saved us. It was the trust we had built with banks, suppliers, and customers. This reflects an important but often overlooked principle: organisations survive crises not merely through strategy, but through social capital.

Looking back, many of these experiences reflect well-known management ideas—but experienced in raw form.

The repeated failures in CEO selection highlight the limits of traditional “rational” decision-making models and reinforce the importance of cultural and behavioural fit.

The strike and crisis management episodes show elements of participative management and adaptive leadership, where flexibility and trust outperform rigid systems.

The lean management failure illustrates the danger of applying best practices without considering environmental uncertainty—what modern theory would describe as a misalignment between system design and supply chain risk.

Finally, the banking episode is a classic example of reputation capital and relationship-based management, concepts that are now widely discussed but were then simply matters of instinct and experience.

Management, I have come to realise, is rarely neat or predictable. It is messy, human, and often contradictory. What works in one situation may fail in another. What appears logical may not work in practice. And what seems like a setback may later prove to be a valuable lesson.

Dankotuwa tested all these dimensions. It was not an easy journey. But it was one that deepened my understanding of management far beyond what any textbook could offer.

And perhaps that is the real lesson: theory explains, but experience teaches.

(Sunil G. Wijesinha is a Consultant on Productivity and Japanese Management Techniques
