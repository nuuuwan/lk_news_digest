Sri Lanka’s freshwater ecosystems in danger

Sri Lanka’s fragile freshwater ecosystems are facing an alarming new threat, with three unique species of rock-dwelling frogs now on the brink of severe decline due to rising temperatures and rapid habitat degradation, according to field researcher Suneth Kanishka.

He warned that these little-known yet ecologically significant amphibians—locally known as “gal para madia”—are highly sensitive to microclimatic changes, making them particularly vulnerable to the dual pressures of global warming and human-driven environmental destruction.

Speaking to The Island, Kanishka said that recent field observations indicate a noticeable reduction in population densities in several historically recorded habitats.

He added :”These species are extremely dependent on stable, cool, and moist rock microhabitats found near streams and shaded forest floors. Even a slight increase in ambient temperature can disrupt their survival.”

He stressed that the ongoing impacts of global warming are intensifying these threats, particularly in lowland and intermediate zones where temperature fluctuations are becoming more pronounced. “What we are witnessing is not just a gradual decline, but a potentially irreversible loss if urgent interventions are not made,” he warned.

Kanishka further noted that habitat destruction—driven by deforestation, land clearing, and unregulated development—has compounded the crisis. “These frogs rely on very specific ecological niches. When forests are cleared or streams are altered, their entire habitat disappears. Unlike more mobile species, they cannot migrate long distances to adapt,” he said.

Sri Lanka is globally recognised for its high level of endemism, with many species found nowhere else in the world. However, conservationists fear that inadequate attention to amphibian species has left them largely overlooked in mainstream conservation planning.

He said that the degradation of these microhabitats is closely linked to broader ecological imbalances. The loss of such species could disrupt nutrient cycling and affect other organisms within the same ecosystem.

Kanishka urged authorities to prioritise conservation measures, including habitat protection, environmental impact assessments, and public awareness campaigns. “If we fail to act now, we risk losing not only these species but also the delicate ecosystems they support,” he cautioned.

He also called for increased scientific research and funding to better understand the distribution and ecological requirements of these frogs. “There is still so much we don’t know. Without proper data, conservation efforts will remain inadequate,” he added.

He stressed that Sri Lanka’s biodiversity, already under pressure from climate change and human activity, cannot afford further neglect. The plight of these rock-dwelling frigs serves as a stark reminder that even the smallest creatures play a vital role in maintaining ecological balance—and their loss could have far-reaching consequences.
