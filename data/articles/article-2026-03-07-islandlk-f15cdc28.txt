Kebiliththa forest slowly recovering from 2021 devastation, but cattle grazing emerging as new threat

The scarred forests of Kebiliththa in Sri Lanka’s Monaragala District are slowly showing signs of recovery nearly five years after large-scale illegal clearing shocked conservationists and triggered national outrage.

Environmentalists who recently visited the area say that although vegetation has begun to regenerate naturally, the forest ecosystem still bears the deep wounds of the destruction that took place in 2021. More worryingly, they warn that illegal cattle grazing has now emerged as a major threat to the fragile regeneration of the forest.

Environmental scientist Hemantha Withanage said a recent field inspection revealed that the forest, which once suffered widespread clearing, is slowly attempting to heal.

“We went to the Kebiliththa forest recently to observe the present condition,” Withanage said. “When the destruction was halted in 2021, around 8,000 trees — locally referred to as ‘Yakkha Puththu’ — had already been cut down. Today we can see that some natural regeneration has begun. But the forest is still very vulnerable.”

The Kebiliththa forest complex lies within the dry-zone wilderness landscape of southeastern Sri Lanka and forms part of a vital ecological corridor that connects several wildlife habitats. The area supports elephants, leopards, reptiles, birds and numerous endemic species that depend on intact forest ecosystems.

The forest gained national attention in 2021 when environmental groups exposed extensive illegal clearing carried out in parts of the reserve. Large areas had been cut down primarily for cultivation and land encroachment, sparking protests from conservationists who warned that the destruction would have long-term ecological consequences.

Environmental activists later revealed that when authorities finally intervened to halt the clearing, thousands of mature trees had already been felled, leaving wide stretches of land degraded and vulnerable to erosion.

According to Withanage, the number of trees cut during the illegal operation was estimated at around 8,000, a figure that illustrates the scale of the environmental damage inflicted within a relatively short period.

“The destruction happened very quickly,” he said. “But restoring a forest ecosystem takes decades. A dry-zone forest cannot recover overnight.”

Despite the devastation, environmentalists say the forest is now showing encouraging signs of natural recovery.

Young saplings, shrubs and grasses have begun to grow in areas that were once stripped bare. Some patches are slowly developing secondary forest cover as nature attempts to reclaim the damaged landscape.

“Early regeneration is a positive sign,” Withanage explained. “But the ecosystem is still in a very delicate state. If further disturbances occur now, the recovery process could be reversed.”

One of the most alarming new threats to the recovering forest is the rapid increase in illegal cattle grazing within the Kebiliththa area.

Environmentalists say herds of cattle are being brought into the forest by livestock owners who allow the animals to roam freely in the regenerating vegetation.

This practice, they warn, is severely undermining the forest’s natural recovery.

“With the forest beginning to regenerate, cattle grazing has become a serious issue,” Withanage said. “Large numbers of cattle are now being brought into the area. These animals feed on young saplings and destroy new vegetation that is essential for the forest to recover.”

He explained that cattle not only consume newly growing plants but also trample fragile seedlings and compact the soil, making it more difficult for native trees and plants to regenerate.

Over time, such disturbances can prevent forests from re-establishing their natural structure.

“If cattle continue to graze freely in these recovering areas, the forest will struggle to regenerate properly,” Withanage warned.

Environmentalists also point out that cattle grazing inside protected forests can have broader ecological consequences.

In addition to damaging vegetation, large numbers of livestock can introduce invasive plant species, degrade soil quality and disrupt wildlife habitats.

There are also concerns that cattle presence in wildlife habitats could increase the risk of disease transmission between livestock and wild animals.

Moreover, grazing inside forests frequently leads to conflicts with wildlife, particularly elephants.

Kebiliththa lies within an important elephant habitat zone in southeastern Sri Lanka. The forests surrounding the region serve as critical feeding grounds and movement corridors for elephant populations.

When cattle are brought into these forests, they compete directly with wild herbivores for food and space.

Environmentalists warn that such competition could further intensify Sri Lanka’s already severe human–elephant conflict.

“Elephants depend on these forests for food,” Withanage said. “When humans bring cattle into the same areas, it creates additional pressure on the ecosystem and increases the chances of conflict.”

Sri Lanka has been grappling with one of the world’s most serious human-elephant conflict crises. Each year, hundreds of elephants and dozens of people lose their lives in confrontations that are largely driven by habitat loss and fragmentation.

Conservationists say protecting remaining forests such as Kebiliththa is crucial if the country hopes to reduce these conflicts.

Sri Lanka’s forest cover has been steadily declining over the decades due to agricultural expansion, infrastructure development and illegal encroachment.

Although official figures often highlight improvements in overall tree cover, environmental scientists note that natural forests — which contain complex ecosystems and biodiversity — are continuing to shrink.

Dry-zone forests like those in Monaragala play an especially important role in maintaining ecological balance.

They act as natural water catchments, store carbon, prevent soil erosion and provide habitat for countless plant and animal species.

When such forests are cleared or degraded, the environmental consequences can extend far beyond the immediate area.

For this reason, environmentalists say the recovery of Kebiliththa must be protected through strict enforcement of conservation laws.

Authorities must ensure that illegal clearing does not resume and that activities such as cattle grazing inside the forest are properly controlled.

“Stopping deforestation was only the first step,” Withanage said. “Now we must protect the forest while it is recovering. Otherwise the damage that has already been done will only worsen.”

Environmental groups have repeatedly called for stronger monitoring of vulnerable forest reserves across Sri Lanka.

They argue that many illegal activities continue largely because enforcement agencies lack adequate resources and coordination.

The case of Kebiliththa, they say, should serve as a national lesson on the importance of protecting natural ecosystems before irreversible damage occurs.

Despite the challenges, the gradual regrowth now visible in parts of the forest offers a reminder of nature’s resilience.

Where bare earth once lay exposed after the illegal clearing, patches of green vegetation are slowly re-emerging.

But scientists caution that true recovery of a mature dry-zone forest could take several decades.

Standing amidst the young vegetation during the recent visit, Withanage reflected on the long road ahead.

“Nature has begun to heal the wounds,” he said. “But it needs protection. If we allow further disturbances — whether through grazing, encroachment or new clearing — we will lose this forest completely.”

For Sri Lanka, a country recognised globally as a biodiversity hotspot, the fate of forests such as Kebiliththa carries profound significance.

The survival of wildlife, the stability of ecosystems and the environmental security of future generations may well depend on whether these fragile forests are protected today.
