Two Sessions, One Xi

Chinese Two Sessions, the most consequential political gatherings in China, have drawn comparatively limited attention this year, overshadowed by escalating military operations in Iran conducted jointly by the United States and Israel following the decapitation of the country’s head of state, Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, just weeks after the violent removal of Venezuela’s leadership. Trump’s decapitation projects appear to continue and could extend to Russia and then China, creating one of the most dangerous precedents in modern political history, where, from the American point of view, success or failure remains uncertain.

China appears to be losing, one by one, its most reliable partners, exposing vulnerabilities in Beijing’s global network that have long been suspected but are now increasingly tangible. Amid these international upheavals, Xi Jinping presided over the Two Sessions in Beijing, which had just begun at the time of writing and will continue until 11 March.

The Two Sessions are intended to articulate domestic priorities and strategic intent, yet they unfold against the backdrop of a dramatic purge of the People’s Liberation Army. This purge expelled scores of senior officers, including Zhang Youxia, vice chairman of the Central Military Commission and a princeling of revolutionary lineage, whom Xi Jinping once referred to as his “big brother,” as well as Chief of Staff Liu Zhenli and numerous commanders across the army, navy, Rocket Force, Strategic Support Force, and People’s Armed Police. Their removal reflects both a tightening of loyalty and a reshaping of the apex of operational command, sending a clear message about the centralization of authority and the party’s supremacy over the military. While official statements emphasized anti-corruption motives, the scale and timing suggest broader aims: compelling absolute loyalty, ensuring the PLA aligns with party directives, and achieving readiness targets ahead of its centenary in 2027.

This concentration of authority carries inherent risks, as vacant senior positions and less experienced replacements could compromise operational effectiveness, particularly regarding Taiwan, where the PLA is expected to secure a “strategic decisive victory”. Taiwan observes these developments with apprehension, interpreting the purges both as a demonstration of Xi’s authority and as an indication of Beijing’s potential assertiveness. In Hong Kong, enforcement under the National Security Law continues to suppress civil and business activity, creating further domestic strain. Overseas, the Belt and Road Initiative faces new uncertainties. The recent Panama ruling, in which a Hong Kong operator lost control of the Panama Canal—a critical artery for global trade—highlights the fragility of China’s overseas infrastructure projects.

Yet AidData at William & Mary University reports that Chinese state institutions have invested in 363 ports worldwide over the past 25 years, totaling US$23.9 billion. Nearly half of this portfolio went to high-income countries, including Australia, New Zealand, Spain, Brunei, and Singapore, reflecting an effort to secure trade networks and mitigate East-West supply chain decoupling. Even so, setbacks such as Panama reveal the limits of influence, illustrating that infrastructure alone cannot fully insulate China from geopolitical realities.

In a striking diplomatic flurry coinciding with the Two Sessions, several Western leaders have visited Beijing this year, signaling a shift in geopolitical engagement. Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney made a state visit in January, the first by a Canadian leader in nearly a decade, securing agreements to lower tariffs on Canadian canola oil and Chinese electric vehicles, while announcing a “new strategic partnership” that both sides described as realistic and interest-based, acknowledging the need to “take the world as it is, not as we wish it to be” when asked about human rights concerns. Shortly thereafter, UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer became the first British prime minister to visit China since 2018, leading a large delegation of business executives and securing commitments to revive a “golden era” of economic dialogue, expand cooperation in finance and manufacturing, and explore visa-free travel arrangements under consideration by Beijing. Most recently, German Chancellor Friedrich Merz travelled to Beijing with senior industry leaders, aiming to reset Sino-German relations. Merz emphasized dialogue and partnership, while candidly acknowledging competitive pressures, engaging with Chinese counterparts on trade, technology, and multilateral cooperation. But Trump’s ongoing “decapitation” operations in the Middle East and Venezuela may not view these developments favourably, potentially complicating his upcoming visit and creating fresh layers of strategic tension between the US and Beijing.

President Donald Trump is expected to visit China in April amid ongoing trade disputes; while the Supreme Court ruled that many of his broad emergencybased tariffs exceeded presidential authority, his administration responded by imposing new global levies under alternative statutory powers, continuing to maintain high duties on Chinese exports. Trump’s rhetoric continues to be unpredictable, alternating between combative and conciliatory, while US military operations in the Indo-Pacific, including maritime deployments near the South China Sea, apply strategic pressure on Beijing.

A subtle but transformative development noted during this year’s Two Sessions is the repatriation of Chinese experts from Western institutions. Senior scientists, engineers, and economists who trained or worked in the United States, Europe, and Canada are increasingly returning to China, bringing expertise in artificial intelligence, biotechnology, and advanced manufacturing. These returnees are pivotal in accelerating China’s technological self-reliance, ensuring that innovation is not merely imported but indigenously adapted and scaled.

In AI specifically, China is now acknowledged as a global leader, with multiple start-ups and state-backed laboratories pioneering applications ranging from logistics optimization to precision defence systems. Some reports, albeit contested, suggest that Chinese-developed technology has been used by Iranian forces to identify targets in Gulf conflicts with minimal civilian casualties.

The Two Sessions have laid out clear economic, social, and technological objectives for 2026. According to the government work report submitted to the legislature, China sets a growth target of 4.5 to 5 per cent for the year, alongside the creation of over 12 million urban jobs. Consumer price growth is projected at around two per cent, the deficit-to-GDP ratio at roughly four per cent, and personal income is expected to rise in step with overall growth. Grain output is targeted at 700 million metric tonnes, and carbon dioxide emissions per unit of GDP are to fall by approximately 3.8 per cent. Policymakers emphasize the expansion of elderly care services and the development of the silver economy in response to an ageing population.

Technological self-reliance is a priority, with accelerated efforts to cultivate domestic science and innovation. Consumer spending will be stimulated through targeted measures, while reforms in foreign investment policy aim to deepen two-way cooperation and ensure national treatment for foreign-funded enterprises. The report further affirms a commitment to a more open economy, particularly in the service sector, alongside a continued focus on cross-Strait peace under the one-China principle and opposition to separatist forces advocating Taiwan independence. Internationally, China pledges to resist hegemonism and uphold fairness, pursuing a foreign policy rooted in independent peace and global partnerships.

The PLA is expected to achieve significant advancements in information warfare, mechanization, and operational projection by 2027. However, the military purge has injected uncertainty into the synchronization of technological upgrades with operational readiness. Vacancies at the highest levels, coupled with less experienced replacements, could impede rapid decision-making in crises, raising questions about the timing and feasibility of Beijing’s more ambitious defence objectives.

China’s global ambitions are further tested by infrastructure setbacks and Belt and Road uncertainties. Despite significant overseas investment in ports and related logistics, the Panama Canal incident highlights the limits of influence. Simultaneously, domestic policy goals—expanding consumer spending, supporting an aging population, and advancing science and technology—reflect an attempt to balance internal development with external projection. The Two Sessions thus serve as a nexus for domestic and international strategy, combining economic planning, military reform, technological advancement, and diplomatic positioning. Still waters run deep.
