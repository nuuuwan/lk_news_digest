Review of “Around the World in Magnets: Sri Lanka Edition”

The authors are two Indians, Dr. Antony Vipin Das and Vidushi Duggal. They set out to explore some of the most important cultural and historical landmarks of Sri Lanka through a very personal lens. In fact, this is the first guidebook that two Indian authors have written for the Indian market which only covers Sri Lanka’s landmarks.

At first sight, the cover page of the book attracts attention and ignites curiosity. What was the idea behind “magnets”? How was it related to Sri Lanka?

As we started reading, it became obvious that “magnets” referred to in the book are much more than their literal meaning. The book consists of 200 different stories born out of nature, culture, heritage, and traditions of Sri Lanka. Rather than observing from a distance, authors actively engaged with local communities, explored places alongside the people, and experienced Sri Lanka through their time in Sri Lanka. This deep interaction adds authenticity to every story shared in the book.

The authors have deliberately moved away from the conventional format of a travel book. Instead, each story is structured into three distinct parts. The first section captures the personal experiences of the authors, mainly describing emotional and sensual moments when encountering each “magnet.” The second section establishes historical and cultural context, explaining why the location, tradition, or experience is important. The third section, “More to Explore,” presents further related facts relating to the main story. This highly interesting structure adds depth to the book.

One of the biggest strengths of the book is how the authors capture the intangible. For instance, it opens with a magnet inscribed with ‘Ayubowan, ‘ a traditional Sinhalese greeting that expresses good wishes for longevity and health. This opening right away reflects the warmth, hospitality, and respect for culture deeply embedded in Sri Lankan society.

From the capital city Colombo, through ancient cities like Anuradhapura, Polonnaruwa, and Yapahuwa, the authors describe these places with veneration. Rather than overwhelming readers with dates and technical details, they focus on atmosphere, and the stillness of sacred grounds with spiritual energy, and the quiet power of history beneath one’s feet. This approach allows readers to emotionally connect with these places.

Equally engaging are the descriptions of Sri Lankan natural landscapes. Misty hill country, winding roads through tea plantations, cascading waterfalls, and dramatic views from cliff faces. All of them were painted with words that evoke the senses.

Throughout the book, the authors capture the emotions of moments well. For instance, in ‘Nuwara Eliya’, the authors say, “Whether it is the strolls along the Gregory Lake or picking fresh strawberries in the farms, the views of the landscape will forever be etched in my memory.” Likewise, what stands out is the authors’ ability to capture moments of movement, thereby bringing the landscapes alive and reminding the reader of their living, dynamic nature.

The book also explores Sri Lanka’s coastal beauty, including Mirissa, Arugam Bay, Negombo, Hikkaduwa and many more. These beaches are portrayed for the communities and lifestyles that surround them. In a scuba diving experience, authors reflect that, “It was a meditative experience. The ocean, with infinite mysteries, was open to us. I have never felt peace like that.” Such reflections encourage readers to think about environmental preservation while inspiring them to travel.

Sri Lankan food is not described merely in terms of taste, but as a cultural narrative filled with identity, and tradition. The authors experienced a wide range of local dishes, including string hoppers, Kiribath, Sambol, Devilled Dishes, Kottu Roti, and many more, savoring flavors that span spicy, tangy, sweet, and sour. In ‘String Hoppers’, for instance, the authors note: “…with the chicken and fish curries and they absorbed the gravy, making them so rich and flavourful. The texture of the string hoppers is quite unique and complements the food perfectly”. Through such stories, readers gain insight into everyday life, hospitality, and the deep sense of sharing that lies at the heart of every Sri Lankan.

Perhaps the most powerful element of the book is its strong focus on people. Throughout the 200 stories, local voices ring out; guides, villagers, artisans, shop owners, farmers, and fellow travelers. It is these meetings of human beings with human beings that give the book its roots. The authors also share insights they have picked up through their guides. For example, under the magnets of ‘Vesak Day’ and ‘Poya Day’, they relate how their guide goes to temples, conducts religious rituals, and distributes alms with his family while depicting Buddhist heritage. The book reminds us that travel is ultimately about relationships and that places become meaningful because of the people who inhabit them.

It is not an easy task to review this book within the limits of a short article as it covers an immense area of Sri Lankan cultural and heritage landmarks. Ultimately, ‘Around the World in Magnets – Sri Lanka Edition’, is not a collection of 200 magnets. It is a tribute to Sri Lanka, its people, and its spirit by Dr Antony Vipin Das and Vidushi Duggal.
