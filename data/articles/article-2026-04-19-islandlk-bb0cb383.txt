Pollution and chemical footprint in the womb

When I recently heard from Dr. S Banerjee about scientific findings showing the presence of chemicals in umbilical cord blood, it left me deeply concerned. A child’s first breath marks the beginning of life outside the womb, yet long before that moment the developing fetus already shares the biological environment of the mother. In today’s world that environment is increasingly shaped by the chemical footprint of modern civilisation.

Across the globe, rivers carry industrial effluents, farmlands are repeatedly treated with pesticides, and food passes through multiple stages of processing and packaging. The air itself contains residues from vehicles, factories, and burning fuels. These activities have certainly helped increase agricultural production and economic growth, but they have also introduced many synthetic chemicals into our surroundings. Gradually, these substances have become part of everyday life.

For a long time, people believed that the womb protected the unborn child completely from environmental hazards. However, modern biomedical research shows that this protection is not absolute. Scientists studying umbilical cord blood—the blood that circulates between the mother and fetus—have found traces of several environmental chemicals that originate from agriculture, industry, and consumer products.

The scale of this exposure is surprising. Researchers estimate that more than 350,000 industrial chemicals and chemical mixtures are currently used worldwide, yet only a small number have been carefully studied for their long-term effects on human health. Biomonitoring studies have detected hundreds of such chemicals in the cord blood of newborn babies, showing how deeply modern pollutants have entered the human environment.

Among the substances frequently found are per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS), often called “forever chemicals.” These chemicals are used in products such as non-stick cookware, waterproof clothing, stain-resistant fabrics, and grease-resistant food packaging. Because they do not easily break down, they remain in the environment and in human tissues for many years. Studies have shown that compounds like PFOS and PFOA can be detected in both maternal blood and umbilical cord blood. Researchers believe that exposure to such chemicals during pregnancy may influence the immune system and metabolism of children later in life.

Another group of chemicals detected in cord blood are polybrominated diphenyl ethers (PBDEs). These are flame-retardant substances used in furniture, electronics, and building materials. Over time they accumulate in indoor dust and may enter the human body through inhalation or accidental ingestion. Some studies suggest that prenatal exposure to PBDEs could affect thyroid hormones and early brain development.

Heavy metals are another cause for concern. Mercury, lead, and cadmium—released from coal burning, industrial emissions, and contaminated food—can cross the placenta and reach the developing fetus. Mercury exposure during pregnancy has been linked to effects on the developing nervous system, while lead exposure has long been associated with learning and behavioural difficulties in children.

Modern plastics also contribute to chemical exposure. Substances such as bisphenol-A (BPA) and phthalates, commonly used in plastic containers, packaging materials, and cosmetics, are known as endocrine-disrupting chemicals. These compounds can interfere with natural hormones that regulate growth and development, and scientists continue to study how prenatal exposure may affect metabolism and health later in life.

It is important to remember that detecting these chemicals does not automatically mean that immediate harm has occurred. Modern laboratory techniques are extremely sensitive and can identify substances even at very tiny concentrations. Nevertheless, their presence in fetal blood shows how closely environmental pollution is linked with human biology.

The developing fetus is particularly sensitive because organs such as the brain, immune system, and endocrine system are forming rapidly during pregnancy. At this stage, even small disturbances in the body’s chemical signals may influence how tissues grow and function. Some studies have linked prenatal exposure to certain pollutants with lower birth weight or subtle developmental changes, although scientists are still working to understand the full effects. These findings remind us that environmental pollution is not only an external problem affecting air, water, or soil. It can also become part of the internal chemical environment of the human body. The first environment a child experiences is not the outside world but the biological environment of the mother.

Recognising this reality does not mean rejecting technological progress. Modern agriculture, industry, and materials have greatly improved living standards and food production. The challenge is to ensure that development continues with greater awareness of its environmental and health consequences.

Safer chemical design, better environmental monitoring, responsible agricultural practices, and increased public awareness can help reduce unnecessary exposure to harmful substances. Protecting the environment is therefore not only about preserving forests, rivers, and wildlife. It is also about safeguarding the conditions in which human life begins.
