Ocean cooling around Sri Lanka signals seasonal shift; DMC on Alert

The ocean waters surrounding Sri Lanka have cooled significantly over the past few days, with colder water moving from north to south along the eastern seaboard and wrapping around the southern coast, according to renowned oceanographer Professor Charitha Pattiaratchi.

Explaining the phenomenon, Prof. Pattiaratchi said the cooling pattern reflected active seasonal ocean circulation in the northern Indian Ocean, largely driven by changing wind systems and currents.

“What we are seeing is a distinct flow of cooler water along the east coast, which then curves around the southern coast. This is a regional-scale oceanographic process rather than a localised or abnormal event,” he said.

The Disaster Management Centre (DMC) said it was closely monitoring the evolving sea conditions in coordination with the Meteorology Department, the Navy and other relevant agencies, particularly in view of potential impacts on coastal communities and maritime activities.

A senior DMC official noted that rapid changes in sea surface temperatures could sometimes be associated with rough seas, stronger currents and sudden weather fluctuations. “While no immediate disaster situation has been reported, the DMC is maintaining heightened vigilance, especially for fishermen and small craft operating off the eastern and southern coasts,” the official said.

Marine experts point out that cooler ocean waters are often linked to upwelling, a process that brings nutrient-rich deep water to the surface. This can temporarily improve fish stocks, benefiting fisheries, but abrupt temperature changes may also stress sensitive marine ecosystems, including coral reefs.

The DMC urged fishing communities and coastal residents to pay close attention to official advisories and weather forecasts in the coming days. “We advise sea users to remain cautious and to adhere strictly to guidance issued by the authorities, as ocean and weather conditions can change rapidly during such transitions,” the official added.

According to Professor Pattiaratchi continuous monitoring using satellite imagery and ocean models will help determine whether the cooling trend will persist or shift as monsoon-related wind patterns evolve.

Sri Lanka’s surrounding seas are known to undergo marked seasonal variations, but authorities stress that timely information-sharing and preparedness remain essential to minimise risks to life and livelihoods along the coast.
