The Art of Preparation: Lessons from Sanghamittā Mahātherī and Mahesī Anulā Devī

In a world that often celebrates “day”, “arrival” and the “result” — the signed treaty, the product launch, the graduation ceremony — the history of the Buddha Dhamma whispers a more profound secret: Success is not an event; it is a prepared process.

Some 2,300 years ago, a spiritual diplomacy changed the face of civilisation in Sri Lanka. It was the arrival of the Saṅghamittâ Mahâtherî, Dhamma Envoy of the Aśokan Empire with the sapling of the sacred Bodhi Tree and the establishment of the Bhikkhunî Sangha.

However, by examining the historical chronicles of the island, we uncover a far greater truth: these events were the result of meticulous, high-stakes preparation orchestrated by two extraordinary Dhamma leaders — the Preceptor in Jambudîpa and the Activator in Sri Lanka.

Their strategy, documented in the historical chronicles, Dîpavasa and Mahâvasa and corroborated by rock inscriptions — present a reality entirely overlooked by devotional Disneyland Buddhism — offers a masterclass in leadership, strategy, foresight, and planning, relevant to every student, CEO, politician, and diplomat today.

Building the Right Team When Emperor Asoka acquiesced to send the Bhikkhunî Order to Sri Lanka, the mission required more than just a figurehead; it required a quorum of excellence. Sanghamittâ Therî did not simply gather friends or random volunteers. She engaged in strategic selection.

She knew that to introduce the Bhikkhunî tradition into a new environment (“foreign soil” as the chronicles say), the planning had to be perfect.

First of all, let’s take a look at her qualifications. The Dîpavasa reports her commitment to the monastic path immediately following her brother Mahinda’s ordination:

At the same time Saghamittâ began to exercise herself in the “Sikkhâ precepts.”

The Sikkhâpadâni — “precepts” or “training rules” — refer to the ethical guidelines that form the foundation of Buddhist moral conduct.

Then the Dîpavasa goes on about Sanghamittâ Mahâtherî’s Qualifications: the diplomatic mission was led by a highly qualified scholar:

bahussutâ Sanghamittâ chaabhiññâ vicakkhaâ catasso tâ bhikkhuniyo sabbâ ca bodhim âharu.

“and learned, wise Sanghamittâ who was endowed with the six (extraordinary) faculties, these were the four Bhikkhunîs who brought each a Bo branch (to this island).”

When you read the text, these two precise terms jump out. These are the terms used to describe her:

Bahussutâ (Pâli: Bahu – much/many; sutta – discourse) translates to “well-versed in the Texts,” confirming her scholarly command of the Tipitaka.

She then put a team of people together: Therî Saghamittâ was accompanied by a highly specialised group of eleven other nuns from Jambudîpa: Uttarâ, Vicakkhaâ, Hemâ, Pasâdapâlâ, Aggimittâ, Dâsikâ, Pheggu, Pabbatâ, Mattâ, Mallâ, and Dhammadâsiyâ.

Therikâ Saghamittâ ca, Uttarâ ca Vicakkhaâ, Hemâ Pasâdapâlâ ca, Aggimittâ ca Dâsikâ, Pheggu Pabbatâ Mattâ, Mallâ ca Dhammadâsiyâ, etâ dârika-bhikkhuniyo, Jambudîpâ idh’ âgatâ. ..These young nuns came hither from Jambudîpa to Lanka to establish the Bhikkhunî Order.

The Dîpavasa says they were experts in Vinaya and the Texts.

The Preparation of the Activator: Mahesî Anulâ Devî Active Waiting and Self-Instruction

While Sanghamittâ Mahâtherî was selecting her team at the royal palace at Pâaliputra in Jambudîpa, an equally powerful, meticulous, preparation was happening in Sri Lanka.

Mâhesi Anulâ Devî, sister-in-law of King Devânapiya Tissa, did not wait passively, twiddling her thumbs.

In the nanny state of the modern world, waiting to be told, we often wait for the “experts” to arrive before project work begins. We wait for the acceptance letter, the funding, or the permission. The Mahesî destroyed this “wait and see” paradigm.

The Mahâvaṃsa records that before Saṅghamittâ Mahâtherî even boarded the ship, Mâhesi Anulâ Devî and her entourage of a thousand had already transformed themselves. They did not wait to be told how to be Bhikkhunîs; they instructed themselves. They attended discourses by Mahinda Thera.

They then discarded their royal finery, shaved their heads, and donned the yellow robes (kâsâya). They moved out of the palace and created their own interim monastery, the Upâsikâ Vihâra.

Practising the Ten Precepts, wearing the yellow robe, disciplined, they waited for the pabbajjâ (ordination), looking for the coming of the Theri.

A word for the student or the professional. Mâhesi Anulâ did not say, “I will become a Bhikkhunî when the teacher arrives.” She effectively said: “I WILL BECOME A BHIKKHUNÎ, SO THE TEACHER ARRIVES.”

In global marketing strategy, we talk about the “demand vacuum”. Mâhesi Anulâ Devî created a “demand vacuum.” Her preparation was intense. An exodus of one thousand women, moving out of royal households into modest living, already wearing robes — would have exerted a gravitational pull on the mission. This was the urgency that Saṅghamittâ Mahâtherî used to brief the Emperor. She had to go.

When the ship finally arrived at Jambukola, it was not a meeting of strangers. It was the coming together of two strategic leaders.

The experiences of Sanghamittâ Mahâtherî and Mahesî Anulâ Devî are not dusty spiritual wall-flowers, bizarre statues, or grotesque artifacts. They are vibrant case studies in strategic foresight.

Whether you are preparing for a final examination or leading a nation, look to these two Dhamma leaders. They remind us that destiny is not what happens to us; it is what we prepare for.

The facts presented above are based on the Dîpavaṃsa (Chapter XVIII) and the Mahâvasa (Chapters XVIII-XIX).
