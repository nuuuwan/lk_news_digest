End of Peacekeeping

We are facing a problem that persists despite endless rhetoric and an accelerating collapse of actionable plans. A new report from the Stockholm International Peace Research Institute (SIPRI), scheduled to be launched tomorrow, 25 May, assesses that personnel deployments to peace operations have declined sharply to 78,633—just over half the number deployed in 2016 and the lowest level in at least 25 years. That figure is not just a statistical dip in an otherwise stable system; it is a measurable contraction of political will, institutional reach and, ultimately, belief in the idea that collective security can still be administered through multilateral peacekeeping at all.

What is being described here is not a temporary funding shortfall or an operational adjustment cycle. It is a structural withdrawal. Peace operations are not collapsing in the dramatic fashion of failed states or defeated armies. They are being slowly starved, re-scoped and politically marginalized, while the language of commitment remains intact. Ironically, governments still endorse peacekeeping in principle, still send delegates to conferences, still repeat familiar pledges, yet the material foundation required to sustain those pledges is being withdrawn year after year. The result is a system that performs continuity while losing capacity.

The UN remains the central pillar of this architecture, but it is increasingly a pillar with cracks that are no longer merely cosmetic. The United Nations, as SIPRI data makes clear, is now operating under sustained liquidity pressure, with delayed or unpaid contributions from major donors producing direct cuts in personnel and operational scope. A shortfall of US$2 billion within a single peacekeeping budget cycle is a political signal that some member states no longer treat collective security obligations as binding commitments. The fact that the system still functions at all owes more to bureaucratic inertia than to strategic coherence.

There is a temptation in policy circles to treat this as cyclical decline, something that can be corrected with renewed political attention or incremental reform. That reading is increasingly implausible. The more accurate interpretation is that peacekeeping has entered a phase of geopolitical incompatibility. The Security Council, rather than acting as a stabilizing mechanism, now reflects the fragmentation it is meant to manage. Veto politics, strategic rivalry and transactional diplomacy have replaced the post-Cold War assumption that consensus on minimum security provision was possible. Even routine mandate renewals have become arenas of obstruction and conditionality.

The consequence is visible in the geography of deployment. Sub-Saharan Africa still hosts the majority of operations, yet it is also the region experiencing the steepest decline in personnel. This is not because the need for stabilization has diminished, but because the system that supplies stabilization is retreating. Peacekeeping has not withdrawn from the most fragile environments because they have stabilized; it has withdrawn because sustaining a presence has become politically and financially intolerable for contributing states. The gap between demand and supply is widening, and it is doing so in precisely those regions where the consequences of absence are most severe.

What replaces multilateral peacekeeping in this vacuum is not neutrality or local self-correction, but fragmentation. The SIPRI analysis points to the proliferation of ad hoc coalitions, bilateral deployments and regionally constrained missions. These arrangements are not inherently illegitimate, but they are structurally different from UN peacekeeping in one decisive respect: they are driven by national interest rather than collective mandate. Rwanda, the United Arab Emirates, Uganda and others now deploy forces abroad under bilateral agreements that reflect strategic alignment rather than universal norms.

Martin Bell, reflecting on earlier cycles of intervention, warned in a different context that war produces only losers and that peacekeeping offers outcomes rather than victories. That distinction now feels almost understated. The more pressing issue is that even “outcomes” are becoming increasingly difficult to produce. When peace operations are reduced in scale, restricted in mandate and politically constrained in the use of force, they cease to function as instruments of conflict management and become symbolic presences in environments where coercive actors face little meaningful constraint. The language of protection persists, but the capacity to enforce protection diminishes.

The failure is not merely operational. It is conceptual. The original architecture of modern peacekeeping assumed that impartiality, consent and limited force could coexist in environments where belligerents still recognized the value of external stabilization. That assumption is now routinely violated. In multiple contemporary theaters, armed actors are not seeking containment but dominance, and they are often indifferent to international presence unless it carries credible enforcement capability. A peace operation that cannot credibly threaten escalation becomes a logistical exercise rather than a security mechanism.

The UN’s experience in recent missions illustrates this erosion. Mandates have become more complex while political backing has grown more fragile. Forces are deployed with expectations of civilian protection but without the political consensus required to sustain robust engagement. The result is a structural contradiction: missions are authorized to stabilize environments they are not adequately resourced or politically empowered to influence. This is not a failure of personnel. It is a failure of design under contemporary geopolitical conditions.

The most consequential shift, however, is normative. The idea that multilateral peacekeeping represents a shared international responsibility is weakening. States increasingly treat engagement in peace operations as optional, contingent on domestic political calculation rather than international obligation. This is reflected not only in funding decisions but also in the gradual narrowing of acceptable mandate language, particularly around issues such as human rights monitoring, climate impacts and gender inclusion.

The SIPRI data suggests a system that is not merely shrinking but decomposing into functional substitutes that do not replicate its original logic. The deployment of ad hoc forces in places like Haiti, or the conceptualization of temporary stabilization forces in other conflict zones, reflects an attempt to preserve operational presence without restoring institutional coherence. It is improvisation under constraint. Whether it is sustainable is doubtful, but more importantly, it signals acceptance that the traditional model is no longer expandable.

What is often left unspoken is that peacekeeping depends not only on money and mandates but on a shared belief in the legitimacy of external stabilization. That belief is eroding. The result is a quiet reversion to a more transactional international order in which security is managed through selective partnerships, bilateral alignments and episodic interventions. It is not an absence of order, but a different kind of order—one that is less transparent, less accountable and more unevenly distributed. Consequently, what is ending is not the existence of missions but the coherence of the system that once made them part of a unified framework. What remains is a patchwork of interventions that may still prevent collapse in specific locations but no longer constitute a global model of conflict management.

Kofi Annan, in his last interview with the BBC in Geneva in April 2018, sounded the alarm about the worldwide rise of autocracies while still expressing the hope that the pendulum would swing back. “There are so many issues,” he said, “that no one country can resolve.” He called himself a stubborn optimist, and it was not a casual description. He had confronted military dictatorships, especially in his native Africa, with a seriousness of purpose few of his successors matched, and he insisted that there could be no security without development, no development without security, and neither security nor development without human rights.

Weeks before his death he was still behaving like someone who believed in ordinary continuity, dancing late into the night in a Geneva restaurant until the owner asked him to leave, respectfully noting, “I know you are the former UN Secretary-General, but still I have to close the restaurant.” He died in August 2018 and was buried in Ghana under the flag of the black star, and, as his wife said at the funeral, was “now back home where you started your long journey.” It is difficult to read that arc now without recognizing how much of the system he represented has been hollowed out in practice, even if it still survives in language.
