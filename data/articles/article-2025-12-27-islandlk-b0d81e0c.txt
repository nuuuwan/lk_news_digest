Digambaram draws a broad brush canvas of SL’s existing political situation

Palani Digambaram is not a politician known for grandstanding or abstract theorizing. As Deputy Leader of the Tamil Progressive Alliance, an SJB Member of Parliament, and leader of the National Union of Workers, his politics has been firmly rooted in the lived realities of Sri Lanka’s hill country communities. In this interview, Digambaram explains why he broke ranks with the opposition to vote with the government on the 2026 Budget, citing long-overdue salary relief for estate workers.

He reflects on the foreign policy direction of the NPP government, the evolving political balance in the plantation regions, and the limits of ideology in governance. From disaster response after Cyclone Ditwah to opposition unity and grassroots development, Digambaram offers a candid assessment shaped by decades of on-the-ground political engagement rather than shifting political alliances.

Q: You voted with the government on the 2026 Budget, which surprised many as you are a member of the opposition. What led you to take this decision?

A:We voted with the government because, for the first time, a government allocated Rs. 500 million specifically for salary increments for estate workers. Even the Yahapalana government, of which I was a member, promised salary increases for plantation workers but failed to deliver. This decision by the current government deserves appreciation.

I made this decision on the very first day President Anura Kumara Dissanayake presented the Budget proposals. On that same day, I met Opposition Leader Sajith Premadasa and explained why this move should be welcomed, as it directly benefits the hill country people. Mr. Premadasa understood my position and informed me that I was free to vote with the government on this matter.

Q: How would you describe the foreign policy direction of the NPP government?

A:Foreign policy is a serious and sensitive matter, especially for a country like ours. We must first focus on regional realities. India is our closest neighbor and one of the fastest-growing economies in the world. It is now the third-largest economy globally and one of the most powerful nations in Asia. This reality must be acknowledged when shaping our foreign policy.

If we look at the Ukraine–Russia conflict, we see the importance of regional geopolitics. Ukraine placed its trust in distant superpowers rather than carefully managing its relationship with its neighboring superpower, and ultimately that support did not materialize when it was most needed.

Sri Lanka has assurances from countries like the US and China, but India remains our closest and most reliable neighbor. We must align ourselves more closely with the Indian economy and increase movement between our two countries, particularly to boost tourism. India has consistently been the first to assist us during crises—during the tsunami, the economic crisis, and most recently during Cyclone Ditwah. Strengthening ties with India should therefore be a priority.

Q: What is the current situation of the Tamil Progressive Alliance?

A:We continue to work at the grassroots level. We contested the last election under the SJB ticket, as the SJB is an alliance and the TPA is a member through a Memorandum of Understanding.

The last election was dominated by a strong public backlash against the old political order, which had become associated with corruption and racism. That wave was so powerful that many prominent politicians, including former ministers, lost their seats, while the NPP secured 159 seats in Parliament.

Despite this overwhelming trend, people in the upcountry continued to vote for us. Now that we are in Parliament, we look ahead to the next election, where I do not believe the incumbent government will secure the same level of support.

Q: The NPP has strong and new representation in the hill country. How do other parties, including yours, compare at the grassroots level?

A:Hill country parties like ours have always fought for the rights and needs of plantation workers. We understand their problems deeply and raise them regardless of political inconvenience.

The NPP, despite having representation in plantation areas, operates within strict party limits. Their MPs can only raise issues approved by the party leadership and must strictly adhere to party policy. They cannot go beyond that framework.

During the Yahapalana period, we fought for land and housing rights for estate workers. I was able to secure seven-perch land plots for 8,000 families. The Indian government funded 4,000 houses, while the Sri Lankan government funded the rest.

There were also legal barriers preventing Pradeshiya Sabha funds from being used for estate sector development. We fought, even against our own government, to amend those laws and passed the necessary legislation in Parliament. It is doubtful whether the NPP could do the same due to party constraints.

Earlier, there were only two Pradeshiya Sabhas in the Nuwara Eliya–Maskeliya district, forcing people to travel up to 65 kilometers for basic services. During the Yahapalana period, I increased that number to six: Kotagala, Ambewela, Maskeliya, Agarapathana, Norwood, and Nuwara Eliya. We also expanded AGA divisions from two to four and established a New Villages Development Authority for plantation regions. I do not believe such flexible, people-focused development would be possible under the rigid NPP structure.

A:People voted for corruption-free governance without racist policies, and the government is currently operating within those expectations. However, President Anura Kumara Dissanayake seems to be carrying the entire burden of governance on his shoulders. Many ministers make false statements, which damages the government’s credibility.

If this government fails, it will not be because of the President, but because of the ministers. Any involvement in corruption or dishonesty would bring this government to an end.

This government rose to power largely through social media. While they currently maintain strong control over social media narratives, it is well known that social media tends to favor opposition forces rather than governments. When governing, crises are inevitable, and the same tools once used against former governments are now being used against the NPP.

The NPP/JVP also made promises they are unable to deliver on. For instance, they claimed petrol could be sold at Rs. 100 per liter if taxes were removed. More than a year has passed, and this has not happened. They criticized previous governments for importing rice, claiming shortages could be predicted and avoided. Have they succeeded in doing so? It appears they are now learning that a country cannot be run solely on ideology.

There is also a narrative that the Opposition Leader can never become President. Unfortunately, some opposition figures promote this idea for personal gain. Similar claims were once made about M.K. Stalin in Tamil Nadu, yet today he is Chief Minister. Sajith Premadasa faces continuous mudslinging. In the last presidential election, had it not been for Ranil Wickremesinghe dividing the vote between the UNP and SJB, Sajith Premadasa could well have been President today.

A:In reality, such unity will not happen. While we genuinely wish for reconciliation, Ranil Wickremesinghe will not allow it. When he was remanded and hospitalized, Sajith Premadasa visited and extended support. Yet afterward, UNP-aligned websites launched attacks against Sajith. This deeply hurt him.

Every time Sajith is willing to move toward reconciliation, the UNP leadership attacks him. Under such circumstances, how can he trust them? He knows he will never be allowed to return to the UNP as long as Ranil Wickremesinghe is in control.

Q: How do you assess the situation following Cyclone Ditwah in your districts?

A:When in opposition, the NPP and JVP criticized every government after natural disasters, questioning their preparedness and relevance. This time, despite warnings from the Meteorological Department and international reports, timely action was not taken.

When landslides occur, lives cannot be saved. But when floods were predicted, people in areas like Gampola and Kandy could have been evacuated. I personally visited Nuwara Eliya, Hatton, Lindula, and Ramboda and witnessed the devastation.

Now the NPP/JVP, having come to power, are facing the consequences of their own criticisms. At a district meeting chaired by the President after the cyclone, I emphasized that people prefer to be rehoused in the same areas they lived in. However, decisions are delayed pending NBRO reports. What is the use of holding land while people continue to die?

India has already provided funds to build houses for hill country communities, but previous governments failed to allocate land. If this government acts decisively, those funds can finally be utilized.

A:Sri Lanka has faced one crisis after another. The Easter Sunday attacks devastated the economy. Just as we began to recover, the pandemic struck. Then came the Aragalaya protests, followed by Cyclone Ditwah.

We must understand the seriousness of our situation. This is not the time for political rivalry. We must put party differences aside and work together to rebuild and stabilize the country’s economy.
