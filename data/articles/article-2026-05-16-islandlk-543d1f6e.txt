Kerala: Left Falls, Not Out

Late Thursday morning, 10 days after the count, Kerala finally heard the referee’s whistle. Top enablers at Indira Bhawan in New Delhi have finalized the selection of the Chief Minister for Kerala, who will govern for the next five years. AICC General Secretary and the party’s in-charge for Kerala and Lakshadweep, Deepa Dasmunsi, announced that the Congress has chosen V. D. Satheesan, shocking many supporters of K. C. Venugopal. But both have accepted the decision and committed to working together, at least publicly. The episode reflects Kerala’s familiar pattern, where internal competition is settled through central authority and public unity follows factional disputes.

Beneath this stability, the political system is no longer shaped by doctrine but by administration, coalition arithmetic, and tightening fiscal constraints. The Congress-led United Democratic Front (UDF) has returned to power after 10 years in opposition, ending the Communist-led Left Democratic Front (LDF) government. The UDF secured 102 seats in the 140-member Assembly, while the LDF was reduced to 35 seats, and the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) won three, marking a clear reversal of the previous mandate. The communists have fallen, but they are not out. This outcome makes it necessary to revisit Kerala’s Left story.

The E. M. S. Namboodiripad-era communist movement and the later institutionalization of the CPI(M) created one of the most unusual political formations in modern history. Unlike communist revolutions elsewhere, Kerala’s Left did not emerge through armed rupture or prolonged insurgency. It sought to achieve success by capturing the state through elections, without using violence. It adopted electoral democracy as its primary battlefield early on, treating the state not as an institution to be overthrown but as an apparatus to be captured and redirected. That decision defined everything that followed. It made Kerala’s communism stable, durable, and electorally competitive, but also gradually transformed it from a revolutionary movement into a governance machine.

In contrast, several communist and communist-inspired regimes elsewhere were associated with large-scale violence and state breakdown: the Soviet Union under Stalin, Mao’s China, Cambodia under Pol Pot, North Korea under the Kim regime, and Ethiopia under the Derg all saw severe repression and mass deaths. Eastern Europe also experienced violent Soviet interventions in countries such as Hungary and Czechoslovakia to maintain control. Similar patterns of communist-linked insurgency and conflict also emerged in Nepal, Sri Lanka, and other contexts, resulting in tens of thousands of deaths and disappearances.

Kerala followed a different path. But constitutional integration comes with consequences. Once a movement stops functioning as an opposition force and becomes part of governance, it begins to absorb the logic of administration. The CPI(M) in Kerala became a tightly organized electoral machine, but over time that discipline turned into rigidity. Governance replaced transformation, welfare delivery replaced structural change, and political conviction itself shifted from practice to narrative.

This shift is also visible in contemporary electoral politics. The contest that led to V. D. Satheesan’s emergence as Chief Minister over internal rival K. C. Venugopal reflects a deeper structural evolution within the Congress rather than a simple leadership change. Venugopal represents the logic of internal party machinery, central coordination, and organizational control, while Satheesan represents constituency-based legitimacy and visible electoral leadership. The resolution of that contest in favour of Satheesan signals a broader shift in Kerala politics: legitimacy is increasingly derived from electoral visibility rather than internal hierarchy.

Yet this shift takes place within a structurally strained system. At an India-focused academic conference held at Stanford University last week, Shashi Tharoor made a blunt observation: if Kerala were treated as a sovereign fiscal entity, it would already be considered effectively bankrupt.

Despite its high human development indicators and global reputation for social welfare, Kerala’s fiscal model is heavily dependent on borrowing, remittances, and central transfers. Salary obligations for government employees, pension liabilities, and welfare commitments increasingly operate under tight fiscal constraints. Political promises continue to expand while revenue growth remains structurally limited. The result is not collapse, but chronic imbalance.

This fiscal tension matters because Kerala’s politics is heavily driven by welfare expectations. Every electoral cycle expands commitments without fully resolving the question of long-term sustainability. Political parties, regardless of orientation, operate within this constraint. The CPI(M) expands welfare schemes under Left governance, while the Congress-led UDF offers competing promises of welfare expansion and subsidy re-calibration. Political identity becomes secondary to distributive capacity.

Within this context, the intellectual foundations of Kerala society deserve closer attention. The reformist legacy of Sree Narayana Guru remains one of the most significant but often under-analyzed influences on Kerala’s political evolution. His central message of social equality, education, and rejection of caste hierarchy did not produce a political party, but it fundamentally reshaped social consciousness. The slogan “one caste, one religion, one God for humanity” became less a doctrinal statement and more a cultural foundation for modern Kerala’s egalitarian self-image.

Narayana Guru’s influence matters because it created a social environment in which political extremism found limited structural space. Caste reform movements, educational expansion, and social mobility altered traditional hierarchies that often fuel radical political ruptures elsewhere. Kerala’s communism therefore did not arise in a vacuum of deprivation; it emerged within a society already undergoing deep social reform. That context partly explains why communism in Kerala did not evolve into violent insurgency as it did in other parts of the world. The space for armed revolution was constrained by pre-existing social reform trajectories.

Over time, however, even that moral and social foundation has been absorbed into routine political language. All major parties now invoke equality, welfare, and inclusion, but these terms function as electoral jargon rather than transformative principles. The Congress operates through alliance management and electoral calibration rather than coherent political positioning. The CPI(M) governs through administrative efficiency and welfare continuity rather than mass mobilization.

The BJP remains structurally peripheral in Kerala but is slowly becoming a more visible third force. Despite repeated and intensive efforts by Prime Minister Narendra Modi to expand the party’s footprint in the state, it has largely failed to convert that push into durable electoral gains. In Kerala, it continues to hit a ceiling of expectations without delivery, unlike in several other states where it has steadily expanded its base.

What emerges is a political system that is stable but intellectually exhausted. The red flag in Kerala has not disappeared, but it has been normalized. It no longer signals rupture; it signals continuity. The revolutionary vocabulary that once defined the CPI(M) has been absorbed into the grammar of governance. Even electoral victories are framed in managerial terms rather than political triumph.

The recent electoral outcome and leadership transition reflect this transformation clearly. Satheesan’s rise to Cliff House, the official residence of the Kerala Chief Minister, is not the emergence of a new political direction but the consolidation of a performance-based governing model. Leadership is now judged by electoral execution, alliance management, and narrative control rather than doctrinal articulation. Venugopal’s organizational strength, Chennithala’s seniority, and Satheesan’s electoral visibility represent three competing logics within the same exhausted system. Meanwhile, the current CPI(M) leadership in Kerala is still shaped by the post-2000 consolidation phase, where administrative governance has increasingly replaced mass mobilization. The fact that visibility prevailed does not signal renewal; it signals adaptation to voter psychology.

However, India’s next general elections are expected to be held by April 2029 to elect 543 members of the Lok Sabha. In just three years, the country will face its most consequential electoral test in over a decade, and it will not only determine the future of national governance but also redefine the limits of political authority, economic sustainability, and political credibility across its states, including Kerala, where politics has already moved beyond doctrine into the harder terrain of arithmetic reality.
