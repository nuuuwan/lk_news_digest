Vesak not on May 1?

There is a sound that haunts me more than any explosion or cry of protest. It is the silence of Vesak. For the first time in living memory, our streets were empty of pandals. No colourful lanterns danced in the evening breeze. No dansals offer free rice and kindness to strangers. The hurried, joyful footsteps of ordinary people — driven by devotion and faith — have vanished. In their place is a nation holding its breath.

This silence is not peace. It is the sound of deterioration. It is the sound of confusion. And if we do not listen carefully, we will mistake the death of our Dhamma island for a quiet holiday.

Let me speak plainly, without political allegiance but with deep gratitude. Two opposition leaders — . Namal Rajapaksa and Udaya Gammanpila — made a decision that should shame every other politician in this country. They chose not to celebrate May Day spectacles. Instead, they gave priority to the Dhamma. In an era where power is everything, they bowed to something higher.

I do not praise them as perfect men. I praise their action. It is a rare lighthouse in a storm of selfishness. Let every party, every minister, every village leader ask themselves: What did I sacrifice for Vesak?

Now, let us move from emotion to evidence. I am a scientist. I deal with numbers that do not lie. Here is the simple, iron-clad calculation of our lunar calendar:

· One full moon to the next (Poya cycle) = 29.5 days exactly.

· One Buddhist lunar year = 12 such cycles = 12 × 29.5 = 354 days.

You do not need a university degree to verify this. You only need a calendar and the courage to look. Yet the government — a government that has shown again and again its contempt for the five precepts (sila), its addiction to corruption, and its cunning manipulation of everything sacred — now dares to alter the birth certificate of the Buddha himself.

If this is true, it is not a policy change. It is a spiritual crime. I struggle to find adequate words for its seriousness.

Think carefully, my dear fellow citizens. If a government can move the Buddha’s birthday for political convenience, what stops them from moving Christmas for Christians? What stops them from shifting Deepavali for Hindus? What stops them from rewriting history, renaming schools, or erasing our very identity?

I am not exaggerating. I am observing. Once respect for the sacred dies, everything becomes negotiable. And when everything is negotiable, nothing is true.

Where is our wisdom? A hard question for monks and lay people

I practice the Buddha’s path every day. I develop kindness (metta) and compassion (karuna) even toward those who insult me. I refuse to let a single word of hatred leave my lips. But I will not betray the truth.

So let me ask, without anger but with deep sorrow: How much real morality, concentration, and wisdom do most of today’s monks and laypeople actually possess? How much general knowledge? How much education that goes beyond memorization?

I travel across this country. I see brilliant minds wasted on partisan loyalty. I see temples filled with ritual but empty of reflection. I see a society that has forgotten how to calculate a Poya date, let alone meditate on anicca (impermanence). The lethargy and despair are not accidents. They are harvests. We planted carelessness. We are reaping foolishness.

Let me say something uncomfortable. A democracy only works when voters are intelligent enough to choose leaders of character. Sri Lanka has proven, year after year, that we are not there yet. Without hatred, without malice, I state the obvious: we have given birth to a state that is extremely low and despicable. Not because of one man or one party, but because millions of us refused to think.

You should understand this consequence. Every vote cast in ignorance is a seed of future suffering. The Buddha taught cause and effect (kamma). Politics is no exception.

I see a government changing the Buddha’s birthday. I see a majority losing their wisdom. I see monks silent when they should roar. I see lay people arguing about cricket while their Dhamma island sinks.

But I also hope. I hope because the Dhamma is not a calendar date. The Dhamma is a living truth. No government can erase it — though they can certainly insult it. And insults can wake us up.

Do not share this article out of anger. Share it out of awareness. Check the calendar yourself. Ask your local monk: When is the real Vesak? Ask your Member of Parliament: Why are you silent? And ask yourself: What kind of Buddhist am I, if I allow politicians to rewrite the birth of my Lord Buddha?

Let us not wait for next year. Let us not wait for lanterns and pandals to return. Let this silent Vesak be the loudest wakeup call in our history.

May the Triple Gem protect us — from ourselves. Yours in the Dhamma,
