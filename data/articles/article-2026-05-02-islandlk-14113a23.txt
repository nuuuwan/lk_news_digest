BALIFICATION: Interior designers in a dying garden

It is not merely a shift in aesthetic; it is a singularity. It is the rapid, ruthless homogenization of a landscape, engineered entirely to cater to a specific socio-economic group that worships one thing above all else: convenience.

It always starts under the innocent, glittering guise of “progress”. A single, genuinely beautiful space opens up with soul and quality, like Citra or Trax. But in a paradise, a good thing never stays quiet for long. The whisper becomes a roar. The “word is out”.

Suddenly, the battle cry of the modern colonizer echoes across the coast: “Sri Lanka is the next Bali! Ahangama is the new Canggu! Hiri is the next Uluwatu!”. A friend of mine aptly calls this migratory circuit the “Red Flag Triangle”—a perpetual loop between Bali, Portugal, and Sri Lanka. The red flags simply pack up their yoga mats and travel between them, demanding the exact same aesthetic wherever they land.

What follows their arrival is an avalanche. A hundred lesser-quality versions of that first cafe spring up overnight. They serve the exact same imported slurry of avocado toast and perfectly poured flat whites. They are packed to the brim because the horde has arrived, and the horde demands the familiar. They want the wildness of the jungle, but only if it comes with high-speed Wi-Fi and cut-cement bathrooms. They want the aesthetic of the third world with the hyper-convenience of the first.

But what happens when you paste a Scandinavian-minimalist, Bauhaus-inspired lifestyle over a fragile, ancient ecosystem? You get a catastrophic disconnect.

This disconnect is baked into the very words we speak. Consider our language; for centuries, it has carried the scars of indoor colonization. The Sinhalese words we use every day for our domestic comforts—putuwa for chair, mesa for table, sapattu for shoes—were handed down to us by the Portuguese colonizers over four hundred years ago. We were colonized indoors before we even realized it.

But what is the colonizer’s word for the niyagala mal (gloriosa superba), the glorious red flame lily? What is their word for the ancient  kumbuk trees, or the great hora trees whose massive canopies cast a sacred, life-giving shade over the rainforest that are the lungs of this planet?

There isn’t one. Because they never cared to learn the architecture of the earth. And neither do the new colonizers.

Instead of gathering under the deep, ancient shade of a  kumbuk tree in reverence, the new wave of expats—cliques from various regions, operating like privileged fraternities—gather in these new corroded metal bars to “drink the piss” and get blind drunk, utterly oblivious to the unique and ancient land they are standing on.

We have suffered a collective loss of consciousness. We all know what a chair is. We know what a perfectly designed bar counter looks like. We are a society fluent in the language of interior design, obsessing over the sterile, curated arrangements of our indoor sanctuaries. But almost none of us know the plants that make up a garden.

Because we cannot name the flora, we do not know the “table or chair” of the earth. We cannot recognize the architecture of the forest. We are breeding a generation of interior designers in a dying garden. We know how to clear the land, pour the concrete, and build a beautiful box to house our egos and all the material wealth that goes with it. But we do not know how to perpetuate the living systems that actually sustain us. We take the aesthetic, but we abandon the ancient Sri Lankan art of shramadana—the act of giving back in gratitude.

And while everyone is busy matching their yoga mats to the decor, the infrastructure groans, buckles, and breaks.

The cruelest joke of Balification is the silent, relentless financial extraction that accompanies it. Every overpriced flat white and every night spent in a sterile concrete villa—complete with a bite-sized swimming pool and a garden choked with the same hegemonous ornamentals—is heavily taxed. The government skims an 18% Value Added Tax, a 1% Tourism Development Levy, and a 2.5% Social Security Contribution Levy. The state is gorging itself on this hollow aesthetic boom.

It certainly does not return to the soil. It does not fund anything as foundational as waste collection. Instead, the burden of this imported, unchecked consumption falls directly onto the earth, transforming the island into a bi-coastal wasteland. In the East, staggering 5x5km trash dumps fester kilometers inland, suffocating the primary dry forests of ancient palu trees. In the South, toxic runoff bleeds directly into the ocean at Habaraduwa. The island is drowning while the treasury swells on its peril.

If the state is collecting millions, how can it fail to secure a single train crossing?

The answer is simple: when you prioritize profitable aesthetics over foundational infrastructure, life itself becomes astonishingly cheap. This systemic rot culminated recently in blood on the railway tracks, where a 23-year-old Russian girl’s life was cut short. Her death pulls back the curtain on the entire charade. It is a tragedy that could have been prevented by a simple, critical fix—a fix I have pitched to multiple governments, only to be met with deafening silence.

The departments of tourism, transport, and public service operate in airtight silos, happily pawning their responsibilities off to the next desk. Nobody cares. Nobody possesses the basic logic, or the moral will, to piece it all together. The only machinery that works flawlessly in this country is the extraction of wealth.

While the system extracts from above, the culture erodes from below. The local village ladies—the true stewards who still hold the ancient  Hela-Vedic knowledge of the earth—are being quietly lured into the cold, fluorescent aisles of the very supermarkets the new “white walkers” wander through. The sovereign memory that kept these women and their communities alive through decades of brutal civil war is being cheaply traded for plastic-wrapped convenience.

This tragic forgetting brings into focus a profound truth my friend Karl, an artist, once shared with me: the real war we are waging is against nature. It is a relentless assault against the birds, the bees, and the gracious forest. And until we stop this war against the earth, we will never stop any of the other wars we wage against each other.

And still, the developers and the greedy capitalists cannot wipe the smirks off their faces. I have watched them in their own venues, dancing entirely to the sound of their own egos while a DJ plays tech house, organic house, or whatever the latest trending genre is to a room completely disconnected from the earth beneath their feet. They think they have won because the cafes are full. They think they are smarter than everyone else because they have stockpiled enough petrol and imported avocados to feed the five thousand on their two-week, post-corporate holidays.

But they have won absolutely nothing. You cannot claim victory when your beaches are landfills and your neglected infrastructure is a death trap. You cannot call it progress when the majestic elephants, the ancient  palu trees, and the coral reefs that breathe life into this island and all of humanity are slowly choking to death on the fumes of our own contempt.
