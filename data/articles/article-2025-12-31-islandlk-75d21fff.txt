Coastal law rollback could lift Sri Lanka’s risk premium, say experts

Any move to dilute Sri Lanka’s Coast Conservation Law by retroactively legalising illegal coastal developments may increase—not reduce—investment risk, governance analysts and environmental experts warn.

Sri Lanka’s coastline underpins key sectors including tourism, ports, fisheries, logistics and coastal real estate. It also functions as critical natural infrastructure, absorbing climate shocks and reducing disaster losses. Weakening its legal protection could expose investors to higher climate risk, regulatory uncertainty and ESG-related financing constraints.

Environmental scientist and Centre for Environmental Justice (CEJ) chairman Hemantha Withanage told The Island Financial Review that regularising illegal developments through fines signals policy instability.

“Markets depend on predictable rules. When violations are rewarded, compliant investors are disadvantaged and regulatory credibility is damaged, he added.

The Coast Conservation Act No. 57 of 1981—strengthened in 2011—clearly defines the coastal zone (300 metres inland, two kilometres seaward) and prohibits development without prior approval. Post-2004 Tsunami buffer zones further restricted construction in high-risk areas. These measures align with global best practice on disaster-risk reduction and climate adaptation.

According to Withanage, many coastal assets now under scrutiny were built after the Tsunami, in breach of these safeguards. “Legalising them after construction transfers future disaster risk from developers to the public and financial system, he said.

Climate stress amplifies the concern. Rising sea levels, coastal erosion and stronger storm surges are already affecting Sri Lanka’s shoreline. Projections indicate extensive coastal inundation by 2100, directly impacting asset valuation, insurance availability and long-term returns.

For lenders and institutional investors operating under ESG frameworks, regulatory rollbacks raise red flags. Weak enforcement may trigger reputational risk, ESG downgrades, and tighter due diligence from multilateral agencies, green funds and insurers.

Sections 14 and 15 of the Act explicitly prohibit permits for projects that harm coastal stability or environmental quality. Analysts warn that diluting these provisions could invite litigation, policy reversals and scrutiny from international financing partners.

Selective enforcement also distorts competition. Luxury hotels charging USD 200–300 per night face minimal penalties, while small operators and coastal communities bear compliance costs. This imbalance heightens social risk—an increasingly material factor in investor assessments.

Public access to beaches is shrinking despite legal mandates to protect it, fuelling community opposition and operational risk. Meanwhile, poorly regulated coastal sand mining has accelerated erosion, undermining long-term asset viability.
