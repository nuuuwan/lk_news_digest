Ceylon Cold Stores absorbs inflationary shocks and climate disruptions to protect market share

Ceylon Cold Stores PLC, the operator of the Elephant House brand, has deliberately absorbed significant inflationary pressures and extreme weather-related disruptions during the financial year 2025/26, sacrificing short-term margin expansions to aggressively defend its consumer market share.

Navigating a severely tested operational landscape, the fast-moving consumer goods giant opted against fully passing on escalating input costs to heavily burdened consumers, maintaining volume momentum across its core beverage and confectionery portfolios despite a deeply volatile macroeconomic environment. This calculated margin compression strategy reflects a broader corporate objective to prioritize long-term brand equity and customer retention over immediate profitability in a highly price-sensitive market.

The domestic manufacturing sector faced a barrage of compounding headwinds throughout the year, driven by a sharp escalation in global commodity prices, particularly across cocoa, dairy, and nut supply chains.

This global price volatility was further exacerbated locally by sweeping domestic electricity tariff hikes and persistent exchange rate fluctuations, which heavily inflated the cost of production. Compounding these structural economic challenges were acute physical climate risks, most notably the severe impact of Cyclone Ditwah. The extreme weather event triggered substantial logistical and distribution bottlenecks while severely dampening out-of-home consumption patterns during what is traditionally the peak seasonal quarter for the beverage and ice cream sectors.

Rather than initiating aggressive retail price hikes that could have fractured consumer loyalty, the company’s retention strategy yielded robust top-line momentum. The group recorded an impressive volume growth of eighteen percent in its beverage segment and an eight percent volume expansion in its confectionery lines.

Elaborating on this strategic positioning in his review within the Ceylon Cold Stores PLC Annual Report 2025/26, Chairperson Krishan Balendra stated, “Margins remained under pressure during the year due to elevated global prices of key raw materials such as cocoa, dairy inputs, and nuts, alongside higher electricity tariffs and fuel costs where the category took a conscious decision to absorb the majority of these price pressures without passing them on to the consumers through retail price increase.”

To support this sustained volume growth and prepare for future demand cycles, the entity simultaneously committed substantial capital injections into its manufacturing capabilities, seamlessly deploying Rs. 4.78 billion in strategic capacity expansion investments during the period. These targeted infrastructural upgrades are designed to enhance production efficiencies and mitigate future supply chain vulnerabilities, complementing the company’s broader operational resilience. By shielding its core demographic from the worst of the economic and environmental shocks while quietly reinforcing its production infrastructure, Ceylon Cold Stores effectively solidified its foundational market dominance as the domestic consumer economy transitions toward a phase of gradual stabilization.

The strategic foresight deployed by the group is further evidenced by its robust bottom-line performance for the financial year 2025/26, reflecting a resilient corporate framework despite the volatile macroeconomic climate. The consolidated group revenue expanded by a formidable seventeen percent, reaching Rs. 184.6 billion, largely propelled by steady topline growth across both manufacturing and retail divisions. This revenue momentum cascaded down to the bottom line, with the group’s profit after tax surging by an impressive thirty-two percent to Rs. 7.89 billion. The supermarket sector, operating under the Keells brand, served as the primary revenue engine, contributing Rs. 146.57 billion, while the manufacturing arm delivered Rs. 38.02 billion. Evaluating this financial trajectory, Chairperson KrishanBalendra noted in his review that the group delivered a strong performance, achieving a “seventeen percent increase in revenue and a thirty-two percent increase in profit after tax in 2025/26,” underscoring the success of data-led retail propositions and aggressive volume retentions in the fast-moving consumer goods segment.

Looking ahead, Ceylon Cold Stores anticipates an evolving consumer landscape and has positioned its capacity expansions to capitalize on the expected economic stabilization. The manufacturing sector envisages stronger growth momentum in the latter half of the 2026/27 financial year, banking on the abatement of global energy volatility and the easing of commodity prices. The beverages category is slated to commission newly acquired machinery to revamp and significantly expand its fruit juice portfolio, while simultaneously pushing its newly launched energy drinks to a broader demographic. Within the confectionery space, the company plans to heavily exploit its newly installed extrusion technology to drive the premium single-serve segment and intends to aggressively deepen its recently established market penetration in Australia. Similarly, the supermarket sector is projected to maintain strong resilience, driven by a rapid expansion of its physical outlet network, an enhanced prepared food offering, and a strategic pivot toward competitively priced direct imports and private label essentials.
