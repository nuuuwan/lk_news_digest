Forever Hero Herath: Remembering a JVP Sahodaraya on the 55th Anniversary

Krakow, POLAND – This year, Easter Sunday on April 5 was a deeply meaningful and reflective day for me. As I contemplated the solemn stillness of the crucifixion of Jesus Christ and the hope of His resurrection, my thoughts travelled far beyond the Catholic churches and quiet streets of Poland—back to the rice fields, dusty roads, and restless dreams of my childhood in the medieval capital of Polonnaruwa, Sri Lanka.

One memory, in particular, remains vivid. It was exactly 55 years ago—April 5, 1971. On that Monday morning, as sunlight filtered through the coconut trees, my geography teacher, Tudor Luzon, and I set out on our usual walk to Palugasdamana Maha Vidyalaya. I was just two days shy of my twelfth birthday—a sixth-grade student unaware that history had already erupted before sunrise.

Coordinated attacks on police stations across the island had begun, marking the start of the Janatha Vimukthi Peramuna (JVP) insurrection. A passing police patrol stopped us abruptly and instructed us to put our hands up and return home. An island-wide curfew had been imposed under the government of Sirimavo Bandaranaike.

This year, the JVP-led government under President Anura Kumara Dissanayake commemorated both the 1971 uprising and the 1988–89 insurrection—honoring the fallen youth as “forever heroes.” Among them, one face remains indelibly etched in my memory: my friend and dear sahodaraya—H. B. Herath of Anuradhapura.

We called each other sahodaraya—brother, comrade. It was more than a political label; it reflected a shared struggle and a shared sense of purpose.

As a child growing up in a rice-farming community with water buffaloes in Polonnaruwa, I could not fully grasp the scale of what was unfolding under the Bandaranaike government. In time, I came to understand that more than 40,000 young lives were lost in the 1971 insurrection—many of them rural youth like Herath and me, hopeful and searching for dignity in a system that often seemed indifferent to our existence.

Herath came from the dry plains of Anuradhapura—the island’s first capital for over 1,200 years—while Polonnaruwa, my home, was the later rice-exporting capital. Today, both stand as UNESCO World Heritage Sites. We were sons of the soil, shaped by agriculture, monsoon uncertainty, and the quiet resilience of rural life grounded in Buddhist culture—though I was born to a Buddhist mother and a Catholic father.

We understood what it meant to grow up far from the privileges of Colombo elites—to study under difficult conditions, to aspire against the odds, and to believe that education was the only bridge between hardship and hope.

Years later, in 1979, I entered the University of Sri Jayewardenepura (USJ) after completing my academic year 1978-79 in the United States through an American Field Service (AFS) scholarship at Perham High School in Minnesota. After I returned to Sri Lanka, my path crossed with Herath on the Sri Jayewardenepura University sports ground. The campus was alive with sports enthusiasm, political energy, intellectual ferment, and youthful idealism.

While I pursued student leadership—as founding president of the World University Service chapter and secretary of the University Sports Council—Herath emerged as a prominent figure among JVP-affiliated student leaders.

H. B. Herath (right) and Amarasiri Samaranayake (middle) at a protest against the White Paper on Education

Despite our different academic paths—he in the Faculty of Arts and I in the Faculty of Management—we shared something deeper than discipline or ideology. We were shaped by the same cultural heritage rooted in two ancient capitals, largely untouched by the colonial mindset that influenced elite urban structures tied to the export-led plantation economy. That divide, in many ways, still lingers today as part of an ongoing class struggle and colonial legacy. It has been central to the JVP’s political narrative of national unity beyond ethnic and religious divisions.

On campus, Herath had a commanding presence—physically imposing yet gentle in spirit. He reminded me of Mahasona, one of the ten legendary warriors of King Dutugemunu. On the volleyball court, Herath was disciplined and composed; on the badminton court, I was far less graceful. But in conversation, he was thoughtful, warm, and driven by conviction.

In 1981, when Education Minister Ranil Wickremesinghe of the governing United National Party (UNP) introduced the controversial White Paper on Education, Herath and the Inter-University Students Federation (IUSF) played a central role in mobilizing opposition. Drawing on strong connections with the JVP and the broader student movement, they organized mass protests around the country.

In January, thousands of students, monks, and trade unionists gathered near Parliament in Colombo. Their slogan—”Kolambata Kiri, Apata Kekiri” or “Milk for Colombo, melon for us”—captured the deep inequality between urban elites and rural students. This was more than a policy dispute; it was a struggle over fairness, access, and the future of students like Herath and me.

In February 1982, Herath invited me to a JVP rally at Hyde Park in support of Rohana Wijeweera, the leader of the movement. That day, I witnessed Wijeweera’s powerful oratory for the first time—magnetic and uncompromising. I glanced at Herath during the speech; his eyes reflected both intensity and hope, as if he truly believed a just Sri Lanka was within reach.

H. B. Herath, a 1980 graduate of the University of Sri Jayewardenepura (photo was taken 1975)

The sustained protests eventually forced the UNP government to withdraw the White Paper.

After the JVP became a formal political party, Wijeweera ran in the 1982 presidential election, finishing third. Following the ethnic riots in July 1983, the government banned the JVP, accusing it of subversion. In August, I left for graduate studies on a Hubert H. Humphrey Fellowship at the University of Minnesota.

Years later, during my doctoral research in Sri Lanka between 1988 and 1989, I found myself once again amid turmoil—the second JVP insurrection, known as Beeshanaya. It was a period of fear marked by targeted killings, attacks on infrastructure, and brutal counterinsurgency. An estimated 70,000 young people were killed or disappeared.

Despite the tense atmosphere, I continued my field research in tea estates in the Nuwara Eliya and Ratnapura districts, where the violence felt somewhat distant. Still, the weight of that period stayed with me long after I returned to Minnesota.

It was later—through BBC News, The New York Times, and other local media outlets—that I learned of Herath’s fate. He had been captured and mercilessly tortured by government forces in the dead of night. Nearly killed by prolonged torture, Herath—still alive—was incinerated along with the wounded Rohana Wijeweera. Each was executed a few minutes apart in the early hours of November 13, 1989. Like so many others, the 35-year-old Herath and the 46-year-old Wijeweera were extinguished prematurely in the shadows of a conflict that consumed an entire generation.

As I reflect on Easter—on sacrifice, resurrection, and the enduring power of belief—I think of Herath not only as a victim of political violence, but as a symbol of something larger that happened in 2024: the democratic elections through which the JVP leadership—under President Anura Kumara Dissanayake and General Secretary Tilvin Silva—captured power for the first time in the presidential and parliamentary elections.

In all this, Herath’s dream of a young generation of rural youth who dared to aspire beyond their circumstances has finally become a reality. He surely believed that justice, dignity, and opportunity should not be reserved for the privileged few. In his own way, he carried forward the legacy of our ancient civilizations, where governance, however imperfect, aspired to serve the many.

My own path led me into academia, public service, and diplomacy. Yet it has been guided by the same ideals we once discussed under the shade of campus trees and across sports courts. If I have spoken for Sri Lanka on international platforms, it is also in memory of voices like Herath. His story is not just one of loss—it is a call to remember and to act.

As Sri Lanka continues its search for reconciliation, justice, and equitable development, we must ask: how do we honor those who believed in a better future? Not through violence or division, but through service, integrity, and a renewed commitment to inclusive governance. That, perhaps, is the truest way to remember a sahodaraya.

On this 55th anniversary, I remember Herath not only as a fallen comrade, but as a forever hero—whose dream of a just Sri Lanka lives on in those of us who continue the journey with humility and hope for a Renaissance Nation.
