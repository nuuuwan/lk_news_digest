Private tuition and poor students

Private tuition is well entrenched in most countries including rich countries like Singapore and Finland, which are believed to have the best education systems in the world. However, private coaching in these countries are well controlled by the government with regard to fees, curriculum, extra subjects that the schools do not provide, numbers in a class, etc. Moreover, private tuition is restricted to higher grades which prepare students for competitive university entrance exams in subjects like medicine, law, and engineering. Poor students, if they want, could access loan systems to pay for private tuition so that they are not disadvantaged and equity is ensured.

This is probably so because of certain advantages that well-structured private coaching have, such as personalised attention, enhanced opportunity for development of communication skills and also targeted support for weaknesses. These advantages are attainable only when the number of students in a class is small and the system is under control and certainly not in mass classes with hundreds of students and conducted via public address system as often happens in Sri Lanka.

There are also several very significant disadvantages in private tuition which could be worsened when the system is uncontrolled. One of the main ills is the fact that extra hours in a private tuition class cost students time for rest and extracurricular activities needed for social development, leading to stress and mental fatigue. When the syllabus is heavy as in Sri Lanka this problem is worse. Another issue is the cost which can exclude lower-income families and strain budgets, potentially making it a luxury rather than a necessity. Students may become too used to instant answers, failing to develop problem-solving skills and independence needed for university and life. Rote learning is encouraged at the expense of critical thinking. These students may find it difficult to cope with the demands of employment and life in general.

An inexperienced or incompetent tutor can cause waste of money, or even make a child dislike a particular subject. In rural areas in Sri Lanka where high fee levying is unfeasible such low quality teachers who don’t charge much may be active. There is also the danger of private tuition being promoted to compensate for the deficiencies in the state education system, instead of rectifying them. It is well known that the teaching in our schools is qualitatively and quantitatively inadequate and unsatisfactory. It is said that some teachers prepare for their private classes during school hours neglecting their students who have travelled far to learn from them.

Private tuition in Sri Lanka starts very early in a student’s life perhaps from year one, certainly from year five in preparation for the Grade Five Scholarship exam. This exam must benefit the bright poor children by enabling them to enter better schools. But here too private tuition often proves to be a disabling factor for poor students, who cannot afford good tutors. The syllabus for this exam is unnecessarily heavy and children cannot reach the required standards without extra coaching. As about 24% Sri Lankans are poor, a substantial number of students would not be benefitted by the very system meant for their benefit.

Children from higher-income families perform markedly better in the exam. Research indicates that upper-income students are disproportionately represented in the highest scoring bands, while lower-income students are in the lower mark ranges.

2016 statistics show that in the Western Province, 13.7% of candidates from higher-income classes scored above the cutoff mark, compared to only 5.8% of those from lower-income classes. This disparity may be greater in other provinces where facilities are less and poverty is high. Moreover, it highlights the influence of socioeconomic status on exam on examination results. The difference is often attributed to unequal access to resources. Affluent families can afford private tuition, which can cost a significant amount of money, and provide enriched learning environments and better school resources. Students from underprivileged provincial schools, which accommodate the poorest, are less likely to even take the exam.

Nationally, only about 10% to 17% of all students who sit the Grade Five Scholarship exam generally qualify for bursaries or gain admission to prestigious schools. To receive a bursary, a child must be from a household earning below a certain income threshold and score above the cutoff, a combination achieved by a very small proportion of the total student population (as low as 3.6% according to a 2017 study).

The data suggest the exam, originally intended to help bright but underprivileged students, often reinforces existing socioeconomic inequalities due to these systemic factors. Studies on the Grade Five Scholarship Examination around 2016-2017 highlight disparities favoring wealthier families, geographic areas, and private tuition, alongside concerns about its high pressure (Chandrakumara C, 2016). Key findings indicate that socioeconomic factors, especially family income and tuition, heavily influenced results, prompting calls for reform to broaden assessment beyond test scores. Statistics from 2016 and 2017 revealed that children from higher-income families performed better, with a larger percentage exceeding cut-off marks compared to lower-income groups, challenging the exam’s original goal of helping underprivileged students. Further these studies indicated that attending private tuition significantly increased the likelihood of performing well, raising concerns about educational stratification and fairness. The intense competition led to significant stress, with many students starting preparation from Grade 3,  despite the exam’s primary aim being bursaries and entry to good schools (Department of Education Reports 2016 – 2017, Institute of Policy Studies).

Another negative factor associated with private tuition is its tendency to worsen the school dropout rate among poor families. Private tuition in Sri Lanka significantly impacts poor students, often forcing them to drop out because they can’t afford the escalating costs, which creates a cycle where they feel they must choose between essential tuition and other needs or risk falling behind, exacerbating existing economic inequalities. Its expense pushes many from low-income families out of mainstream education or leads them to engage in child labour to fund it. The cost of private tuition, along with other school expenses, becomes insurmountable for poor families, forcing children to work instead. Further students and parents may believe private tuition is essential for examinations, leading them to drop out of school when they can’t afford it, seeing it as the only path to success.

Poor families are struggling to cover basic education costs, including tuition fees, books, and data for online learning. Some parents feel public schools offer insufficient quality, driving demand for private tuition and making it seem indispensable (Mayadunne G, and Kariyasekera K, 2021).

To resolve these crises what needs to be done, apart from addressing poverty, is to improve the standards of rural schools, cut down the unnecessary stuff in the curricula, do away with the year five scholarship which anyway would be made redundant when the rural schools are improved and ban private tuition for students below GCE O/Level and also regulate and control it regarding fees, curricula and numbers in classes for higher grades. If the education and health of the poor people, who comprise 24% at present, are neglected there is no hope for this country.
