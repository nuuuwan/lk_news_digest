‘Just a Phase’ The Forgotten Lunar Calendar

The International Centre for Ethnic Studies (ICES) will premiere its latest documentary, ‘Just a Phase’, on Friday, 15th May. The 59-minute documentary feature, directed by Sharni Jayawardena, will be screened at the PVR Cinema, One Galle Face, at 5 p.m.

The film explores an extraordinary yet largely forgotten episode in Sri Lanka’s post-independence history. From January 1966 to September 1971, Ceylon replaced the Saturday–Sunday weekend with the Pre-Poya and Poya days, which marked the four principal phases of the moon. This dramatic shift from the Gregorian to a lunar calendar was enacted through the Holidays Act No. 17 of 1965. The new calendar reshaped the rhythms of work, worship, and everyday life across the island.

Through archival research, interviews, and historical reflection, Just a Phase examines how a legislative act that may appear eccentric today was embedded in broader questions of nationhood, religion, and postcolonial identity. Indeed, the shift to a lunar calendar was shaped by the politics of post-independence Ceylon and tied to the restoration of traditions displaced under colonial rule, an effort closely associated with the Buddhist revivalist movement.

Introduced at a time when political parties were competing for the backing of influential Buddhist constituencies, the Act reflected the growing link between religion and electoral politics. Inevitably, the Holidays Act No. 17 also raised questions about whether it represented cultural revival, political expediency, or an attempt to redefine the state’s identity.

The new calendar had a tangible impact on everyday life. Planning became difficult, business schedules fell out of step with the rest of the world, and religious minorities, particularly Christians accustomed to Sunday worship, were compelled to adapt. At the same time, the reform was welcomed by some as the long-overdue revival of a precolonial tradition through the recognition of the significance of Poya days in the island’s religious and cultural life. The temporal experiment ended quietly in 1971, with Sundays, along with full-moon days, reinstated as public holidays.
