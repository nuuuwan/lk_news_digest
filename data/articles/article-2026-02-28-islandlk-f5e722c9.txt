Was controlling local administration SWRD’s long term strategy?

A few days ago, while walking through the Bandaranaike Museum at the BMICH, I paused before a modest but telling artifact: a briefcase labelled “Minister of Local Government,” once belonging to the late S. W. R. D. Bandaranaike. Among the many ceremonial objects and political relics displayed there, it seemed almost ordinary. Yet it lingered in the mind.

That briefcase was not merely an office accessory. It represented a portfolio he held for over a decade, a position that brought him into close contact with the rural heart of Ceylon. Standing there, one could not help but wonder whether Local Administration was simply another ministerial appointment, or whether it formed part of a longer and more deliberate political design.

S. W. R. D. Bandaranaike first rose to island-wide prominence when he entered the First State Council of Ceylon in 1931 as Member for Veyangoda, elected unopposed. A famous photograph published in The Ceylon Daily News showed him in national dress beside his father, Sir Solomon Dias Bandaranaike, clad in Edwardian style, standing at the foot of the State Council building. It was not admiration but ridicule. From the beginning, he stood between two worlds.

According to James Manor in Expedient Utopian, during his first term Bandaranaike “hankered impatiently” after ministerial office. He served on the sub-committee on Local Government led by Minister Charles Batuwantudawe, who, as reported by the press at the time, was considered ineffective, with allegations of corruption raised against him. In the Second State Council, Bandaranaike secured what he desired. He became Minister of Local Administration in 1936, handling Local Government, Lands, Settlement and Survey. It was an important portfolio. It brought him into direct contact with rural Ceylon.

He remained a Minister for eleven years, serving alongside D. S. Senanayake, John Kotelawala, C. W. W. Kannangara and D. B. Jayatilaka. Compared to his colleagues, what major development scheme bears his imprint from that long ministerial tenure? D. S., apart from leading the freedom struggle, did much for irrigation and land reclamation in the North Central Province and Gal Oya.

Sir John was an effective Minister of Public Works and Transport. Some of the structures constructed during his tenure remain strong and formidable. Kannangara is credited for his educational reforms, especially lobbying for the Bill on Free Education. On the other hand, for what was Bandaranaike remembered?

He was a silver-tongued orator. He was charismatic. But he was not methodical. His Oxford training did not translate into administrative brilliance. He would arrive at his ministerial office late and complete his work at a rapid pace, but without lasting effect. As B. P. Peiris, the Cabinet Secretary, records in his memoirs, punctuality and disciplined office routine were not his strengths. In essence, he was more a politician than an administrator.

When constitutional reforms were introduced in the parliamentary system in 1947, D. S. Senanayake was elected Prime Minister and S. W. R. D. Bandaranaike was offered the portfolio of Finance, which was ultimately accepted by J. R. Jayewardene. Bandaranaike declined it. Instead, he insisted on retaining Local Government. That decision was not accidental. As J. L. Fernando, the Lake House journalist, observed, Bandaranaike used that office to organize the rural backbone of the country. He cultivated local bodies. He mobilized support among Ayurvedic physicians, who carried immense influence within village communities. He built networks quietly.

Less than a decade later, under the banner of the Mahajana Eksath Peramuna, he swept the polls. The very rural masses he had courted during his years in Local Administration carried him to power.

Was Banda a competent leader? If we measure him as an administrator, the record is not impressive. Fifteen years in office as a Minister and three years as Prime Minister produced little that was structurally transformative. He lacked method and consistency. His indecisiveness during the 1958 anti-Tamil riots illustrates this weakness. Tarzie Vittachi writes at length on this in his work Emergency ’58. His Cabinet was divided, and by 1959 it was in disarray.

If we measure him as a political strategist, however, Bandaranaike scored high points. He understood the pulse of rural Ceylon long before others did. He built patiently. He waited. And when the moment came, he knew exactly where his strength lay.

He was not a master builder of institutions. He was a master builder of political momentum. This is what made him Prime Minister in 1956 with the “popular trend” of “Sinhala Only” then. The political fire he helped ignite did not remain contained. It consumed him. When his “policies” and momentum failed, he was assassinated by a monk (සඟ) who was a physician (වෙද) and teacher (ගුරු).

Fernando, J.L., (1965), Bandaranaike Legacies, The Times of Ceylon Press

Manor, James, (1989), Expedient Utopian: Bandaranaike and Ceylon, University of Cambridge Press

Peiris, B.P., (2007), Memoirs of a Cabinet Secretary, Sarasavi Publishers
