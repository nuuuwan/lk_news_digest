SL’s apparel exports record softer drop in April as annual projection falls below US$ 5bn

Sri Lanka’s apparel sector witnessed a softening in its April performance, with total exports declining by a softer 4.72 percent to US$ 328.15 million, compared to US$ 344.40 million in the corresponding month of 2025.

Cumulatively for the first four months of 2026, export earnings contracted by 7.47 percent to US$ 1.53 billion, reflecting continued pressure across major export destinations. With these persisting challenges, the industry’s annual earnings are projected to fall short of the crucial US$ 5 billion milestone.

Speaking to Mirror Business, Joint Apparel Association Forum (JAAF) Chairman Felix Fernando confirmed the adjusted outlook, noting, “It will be a little less than $5 billion, around 4.8 to 4.9 billion.”

The contraction is largely a reflection of suppressed demand in traditional strongholds, as widespread economic pressures dampen consumer spending. This suppressed demand is starkly visible in the performance of major global apparel brands that source heavily from Sri Lanka, including Victoria’s Secret, Nike, Tommy Hilfiger, Calvin Klein, and Marks & Spencer. These industry giants are currently navigating a cautious retail environment characterized by tightened inventory and softer consumer appetites. For instance, recent financial metrics from leading sportswear brands like Nike indicate expected revenue contractions of around 5 percent and squeezed margins in early 2026, as digital sales and store traffic face global headwinds.

Compounding the issue in the United States, recent economic data indicates that U.S. consumer confidence hit a rock bottom in May 2026 amid persistent inflation and economic uncertainty. This dampened consumer sentiment directly translates to reduced spending on discretionary items like apparel, evidenced by a steep 12 percent drop in overall apparel imports to the U.S. from all sourcing countries in the first quarter. Reflecting this broader trend, Sri Lanka’s direct exports in April saw the UK down by 16.91 percent, the EU falling 8.78 percent, and the U.S. slipping 3.46 percent. Fernando attributed the dip to a confluence of global headwinds. “Global recession, Trump taxes, and the Iran war are the main reasons for the dip,” he explained, adding that it is a “temporary market dip due to the Iran war, as the first to cut down is non-essential items.”

In Europe, the challenge of lowered demand is exacerbated by fierce regional competition from Vietnam and Bangladesh. “In Europe, people can’t understand this, but there is a lot of import coming from Vietnam and Bangladesh,” Fernando stated, highlighting a critical structural disadvantage regarding trade concessions. “Less than 60 percent (of Sri Lankan apparel) qualified for GSP Plus due to fabric origin, which doesn’t apply for Bangladesh.” Despite the overarching decline, isolated pockets of resilience remain, with local manufacturers like Omegaline recording a marginal uptick. Meanwhile, potential geopolitical shifts in the U.S. could inadvertently offer a silver lining. Addressing the prospect of new trade policies, Fernando suggested that a “Trump tariff could put Sri Lanka on par with Vietnam if it levels it up.”

To mitigate the volatility of traditional destinations, JAAF is heavily emphasizing market diversification to support the sector. While core markets stumbled, exports to other markets recorded a 12.61 percent growth during April, signaling that there is still room to build momentum through greater exploration. “We should be able to do more in the UK and European developing countries,” Fernando emphasised.
