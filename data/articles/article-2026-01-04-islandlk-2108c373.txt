Cyclone Ditwah exposes growing climate threat to Sri Lanka’s endemic flora

Cyclonic Storm Ditwah has once again underscored the increasing vulnerability of Sri Lanka’s unique biodiversity to climate-driven extreme weather events, with scientists warning that many endemic flowering plants—already confined to narrow ecological niches—are facing heightened risks from floods, landslides and habitat disturbance.

Sri Lanka is globally recognised for its rich plant endemism. According to the National Red List (2020), the island is home to 3,087 indigenous angiosperm species belonging to 186 plant families, of which 863 species are endemic. These plants, ranging from towering forest trees to delicate herbs, climbers and epiphytes, are often restricted to specific climatic, altitudinal and microhabitat conditions, making them particularly sensitive to sudden environmental shocks.

Senior Professor Siril Wijesundara, Research Professor at the National Institute of Fundamental Studies (NIFS), told The Island that cyclonic events, such as Ditwah, represent “a serious and growing challenge to the long-term survival of Sri Lanka’s endemic flora.”

“Many of our endemic flowering plants occur in very limited geographical areas and within narrow ecological tolerances,” Prof. Wijesundara said. “When extreme rainfall, landslides or prolonged flooding affect these habitats, the impact can be irreversible, leading to local or even total extinction of species that exist nowhere else in the world.”

Research carried out in 2016 by Sri Lankan scientists, using Geographic Information System (GIS) techniques, highlights the gravity of the situation. By analysing more than 84,000 plant location records across 2,847 grid cells of 5 km × 5 km, researchers identified nine major endemic areas: the Central Highlands, South Western Wet Zone, Northern Highlands, Eastern Highlands, Ritigala, Dolu Kanda, Yala, Wilpattu and Jaffna.

Among these, the wet zone and central highlands show the highest concentrations of endemic flowering plants. Core endemic areas such as Sinharaja, Adam’s Peak, Knuckles, Horton Plains and Kandy stand out for their exceptional species richness and the presence of endemic genera including Doona, Stemonoporus, Hortonia, Schumacheria and Loxococcus—many of which are almost entirely confined to wet-zone forests.

It is these very regions that were most affected by Cyclone Ditwah.

Ditwah, which developed in the Bay of Bengal, brought intense rainfall, strong winds and widespread flooding across large parts of the island. In the Central Highlands, prolonged rain triggered landslides and severe soil erosion in montane forests such as Knuckles, Horton Plains and Adam’s Peak. Scientists warn that landslides can strip entire slopes of vegetation, wiping out endemic plant populations confined to small altitudinal belts.

In the South Western Wet Zone, including areas around the Sinharaja World Heritage Site, flooding and prolonged soil saturation have stressed endemic understory plants, while wind damage to the forest canopy has altered microclimatic conditions crucial for regeneration.

Prof. Wijesundara also drew attention to a less visible but equally serious consequence:

“Disturbed habitats created by landslides and floods are highly vulnerable to invasive alien species. Once invasives establish, they can permanently alter forest structure and outcompete endemic plants, posing a long-term threat that extends well beyond the cyclone itself.”

Experts stress that integrating biodiversity data with disaster risk information is now essential. Overlaying maps of endemic flowering plant distributions with GIS-based data on rainfall intensity, flood extent and landslide susceptibility can help identify areas where biological richness coincides with high disaster exposure.

Such analyses, scientists say, can guide post-cyclone monitoring, habitat restoration, land-use planning and climate adaptation strategies, ensuring that development does not further endanger sensitive endemic zones.

Sri Lanka’s endemic flowering plants are an irreplaceable natural heritage. Cyclone Ditwah has highlighted how closely climate disasters and biodiversity loss are now intertwined. As extreme weather events become more frequent, conservationists argue that science-based, spatially informed planning is vital to safeguard the country’s unique flora for future generations.
