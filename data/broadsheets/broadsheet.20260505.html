Parliament received a Bill to widen VAT, bring cross‑border digital services into scope, and raise the rate on financial services to 20.5%.

The draft law cut the standard registration threshold to Rs. 36 million a year from Rs. 60 million, pulling more small and medium enterprises into the net at a time when headline inflation had re‑accelerated above the Central Bank’s 5% midpoint target. It also proposed that non‑resident platforms supplying consumers in Sri Lanka register and account for tax, a change that would end the de facto zero‑rating of popular apps and streaming services and align the regime with peers that have already moved to destination‑based taxation of digital consumption.

The measure followed a sharp rise in consumer prices driven by higher energy costs linked to the Middle East conflict, with year‑on‑year inflation in Colombo hitting 5.4% in April after averaging about 2% in the prior quarter. While the Inland Revenue Department set a record Rs. 2,244 billion collection in 2025 and is tasked with Rs. 2,402 billion in 2026, the draft also tightened compliance: penalties were scaled up to Rs. 1 million and secured point‑of‑sale systems were mandated to close leakages that had widened during the crisis years.

By expanding the base while lifting the effective charge on banks and finance to 20.5% from 18%, the package shifted a larger burden onto sectors that had remained relatively profitable during the downturn. Zero‑rating for specific export‑linked service niches such as garment buying offices was retained, but the inclusion of digital platforms mirrored reforms across ASEAN and the OECD in recent years, with Sri Lanka moving from laggard to late‑mover status on this front.

IFC agreed to finance part of Sri Lanka’s first 100 MW utility‑scale solar plant in partnership with WindForce PLC.

The package included up to $18 million in local‑currency lending backed by the IDA21 Private Sector Window, alongside advisory support on engineering, grid resilience and cybersecurity to help integrate an expected 220 GWh of annual generation—roughly 1.4% of current electricity demand—at a lower levelised cost than imported thermal. It marked IFC’s first infrastructure energy deal in Sri Lanka since the crisis and the country’s first energy transaction in over a decade, meeting a gap that had left renewables largely confined to rooftop and small hydro.

The project design left room for future battery storage to stabilise variability, consistent with the Government’s 2030 target of 50–70% renewable penetration, and was expected to create more than 3,000 jobs during construction and operations. With electricity among the region’s costliest at 15,000–16,000 GWh consumption annually, shaving imported fuel use even by a percentage point improves both the balance of payments and tariff headroom.

By codifying the authority to levy sanctions on tardy or inaccurate reporters, the Bank moved closer to international statistical practice, where enforcement teeth are a prerequisite for timely macroeconomic monitoring. The package also underscored the political support for strengthening technocratic functions: the President, as Finance Minister, sponsored the submission while the Governing Board met statutory deadlines for audited reporting for the first time in several years.

A Government committee submitted a framework to enable voting by more than 1.5 million Sri Lankans overseas.

The report to the Public Administration Ministry proposed legal changes and operational options—embassy‑based voting, postal ballots and secure online methods—for migrant workers, students, dual citizens and others abroad, together with digital registration and updated campaign‑spending rules. The exercise, completed within three months, marked the first time the State had a consolidated plan after decades of discussion that produced no implementable model.

Officials said the draft would go to Cabinet before public consultation, and also suggested exploring advance or mobile voting domestically for essential‑service workers, fishermen, the bedridden and prisoners.
