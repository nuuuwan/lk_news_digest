Sri Lanka and the Maldives signed seven cooperation agreements covering defence, education, tourism, health and archives.
The instruments were exchanged at the Presidential Secretariat in Colombo during the State visit of President Mohamed Muizzu, marking 61 years since formal ties were established in 1965 and signalling a shift from ad hoc engagement to institutionalised sector partnerships. The package included counter‑terrorism and defence education pacts, twin‑destination tourism promotion and university links, alongside reciprocal land allocation to house each other’s missions in Colombo and Malé,
an indicator of long‑term diplomatic intent rather than transactional diplomacy. Trade was framed as a priority: among South Asian Association for Regional Cooperation markets, the island neighbour ranks as one of the larger buyers of Sri Lankan goods, and a possible entry by Bank of Maldives was flagged as a way to ease payments and deepen commercial flows that have historically moved through third countries. Maritime cooperation
featured prominently because both economies rely on Indian Ocean shipping lanes; agreements to curb illicit use of sea routes and to explore fisheries processing and boat‑building tie into existing domestic value chains rather than creating parallel ones. Climate advocacy was elevated to a regional plank, with both governments committing to use multilateral forums to press for finance and adaptation reflective of small‑island exposure to sea‑level rise and weather shocks.
Consumer prices in Colombo accelerated to 5.4% year‑on‑year in April, up from 2.2% in March.
The jump, measured by the Colombo Consumer Price Index, was driven by a sharp rise in non‑food items to 6.8% and a pickup in food to 2.8%, reversing three consecutive months of sub‑3% readings and pushing the month‑on‑month change to 2.99% after a 0.25% increase previously. The Central Bank noted the outcome stays within its 5% target band of 3–7%, but flagged elevated uncertainty from the Middle East war that has tightened global energy markets and filtered into transport costs locally. Core inflation rose to 2.8% from 2.5%, indicating underlying pressures had begun to firm from the near‑zero trough seen in late 2025.
Parliament received a Bill to widen Value Added Tax, lower the registration threshold and tax cross‑border digital services from July.
The proposal reduces compulsory registration to Rs. 36 million annually and Rs. 9 million per quarter—from Rs. 60 million and Rs. 15 million—bringing more small and medium traders into the net while raising the financial services VAT to 20.5% from 18%, increasing the sector’s tax burden relative to last year. Overseas platforms supplying services to users in the country must register and charge VAT, except on supplies to VAT‑registered businesses supported by documentation, aligning the treatment of foreign sellers with domestic providers. Retailers are required to move to secured point‑of‑sale systems on a timetable set by the Inland Revenue, a compliance step that is expected to improve invoice traceability compared with manual receipts.
The Asian Development Bank cut its 2026 growth outlook for developing Asia to 4.7% and lifted inflation to 5.2%.
The revisions—down from 5.1% growth and 3% price gains—were attributed to sustained energy and freight disruption from the Gulf conflict, with oil assumed to average about $96 per barrel this year versus sub‑$70 levels earlier in the year. Under a worse‑case escalation where critical energy facilities suffer more damage, the institution projected growth as low as 4.2% and inflation as high as 7.4%, a combination that would squeeze import‑dependent economies with limited fiscal buffers compared to 2025. The lender urged targeted, time‑bound support to vulnerable households instead of broad subsidies, a shift from blanket reliefs that previously widened deficits without improving energy efficiency.
The International Finance Corporation agreed to finance a 100‑megawatt solar project with WindForce via a local‑currency loan of up to $18 million.
The investment, supported by the IDA Private Sector Window Local Currency Facility, will help build Sri Lanka’s first utility‑scale photovoltaic plant with provision for future battery storage, adding an estimated 220 gigawatt‑hours a year to the grid and creating more than 3,000 jobs, according to the lender. Advisory support on engineering, grid resilience and cybersecurity accompanies the financing, complementing the World Bank’s renewable integration program rather than operating as a standalone asset.
Russia’s Health Minister began a three‑day visit for talks on pharmaceuticals, medical equipment, training and academic links.
The delegation’s programme included meetings with counterparts from health and foreign ministries, tours of the National Hospital and the University of Colombo’s medical faculty, and discussions on Ayurveda and health tourism, a sectoral agenda that has focused on functional cooperation rather than treaty‑level commitments in past exchanges.
Police detained more than 120 foreign nationals in Rajagiriya over suspected financial fraud operations spanning multiple floors of an apartment complex.
Laptops and mobile phones were seized for forensic analysis, with the group including citizens of China, Vietnam, Malaysia, Madagascar, the Philippines, Thailand and Cambodia; several suspects fled during the raid and are being sought, an enforcement action that follows earlier cases in which transnational cyber‑scam cells used short‑term rentals instead of registered offices.
Construction activity remained in expansion in March with a Total Activity Index of 57.1, down from 70.3 in February.
New orders and employment continued to grow but at a slower pace, while suppliers’ delivery times lengthened notably amid transport bottlenecks linked to the Middle East conflict and reported difficulties sourcing fuel and inputs; expectations for the next quarter stayed above 50, reflecting a pipeline of work even as costs and logistics stiffened compared to early‑year conditions.
The head of state convened a high‑level review on introducing electric suburban rail services on three key corridors around the capital.
The first phase under the Colombo Suburban Railway Project would run on the Kelani Valley, Main Line to Ragama, and Coastal Line to Panadura, with funding options, infrastructure upgrades and a possible shift from broad to standard gauge examined, a move that would modernise commuter capacity relative to the current diesel fleet if procurement and rights‑of‑way are secured on schedule.
Maldives’ president arrived for a two‑day State visit that included a ceremonial welcome, bilateral talks and a business forum in Colombo.
The programme featured the exchange of cooperation documents across several sectors and discussions on trade imbalance and investor entry, building on a prior visit by Sri Lanka’s leadership to Malé in 2025 rather than inaugurating ties from scratch; the itinerary also underscored plans to improve air and maritime links that underpin twin‑destination tourism compared with single‑market itineraries.

Sri Lanka rose five places to 134th in the 2026 World Press Freedom Index published by Reporters Without Borders.
The improvement from 139th a year earlier still left the media environment classed as problematic, with concerns documented over national security laws and the Online Safety Commission created in January 2024, whose powers include content takedowns and access to sources that can chill reporting compared with peer jurisdictions with stronger whistle‑blower protection. The index noted more than half of countries globally fell into the difficult or very serious categories, a deterioration versus 2002 when the ranking began, underscoring that incremental gains domestically occurred against a broadly worsening backdrop rather than a global rebound.
A committee proposed legal and operational mechanisms to enable out‑of‑country voting for an estimated 1.5 million citizens abroad.
The report to the Public Administration Ministry set out voter registration via missions, campaign rules under the Election Expenditure Act, and options for in‑person balloting at embassies, postal voting or secure online systems, a package to be taken to Cabinet after consultations instead of piloting ad hoc arrangements as in past exercises. The draft also encouraged exploring advance or mobile voting domestically for essential services personnel and bedridden voters, extending access beyond the diaspora relative to prior cycles.
Rules empowering the Central Bank to compel statistics from external parties were approved for submission to Parliament, alongside its 2025 report.
The gazetted framework under the 2023 Act clarifies collection scope, confidentiality and administrative sanctions for non‑compliance, replacing scattered directives with a single rulebook so that monetary policy and financial stability work are not hampered by delayed or incomplete data compared with the ad hoc requests used previously. The annual operational and audited financial statements for last year were cleared for tabling, keeping statutory timelines intact after the governance overhaul that split monetary and fiscal roles more explicitly than under the old law.
