You will be given several Sri Lankan news articles from the past week. Write a concise, cohesive editorial digest in the style of *The Economist*, output as a JSON list. Each list element must be a dict of the form:

```json
{
  "title": "...",
  "body": "..."
}
```

**Length:** The complete digest should contain exactly 10 items. Each item should have a body of 2–3 sentences, resulting in a total of roughly 800–1000 words across all body paragraphs combined.

**Titles and body as a single continuous narrative:** Write each item so that the title is the opening sentence and the body continues directly from it — together they form one unbroken piece of prose. The title must end with a full stop. No fact, figure, or proper noun introduced in the title should be repeated in the body. The body picks up where the title leaves off, adding new information only.

*Example of the intended style:*
> **Title:** Sri Lanka and the IMF reached a staff-level agreement on the fifth and sixth reviews of the four-year Extended Fund Facility.
> **Body:** The deal unlocked roughly $700m, lifting total disbursements under the programme to approximately $2.4bn, subject to Executive Board approval.

**Tense:**
- Use the **past tense** for all events that have already occurred.
- Use the **future tense** only for events explicitly scheduled or confirmed to occur after the date of the source articles.
- Do not use the present tense to describe past events (e.g. write "the government passed" not "the government passes" or "the government has passed").

**Titles** must read like headlines from a reputable newspaper (think *Financial Times* or *The Economist*) — precise, active, and newsy — but when read in sequence, they should form a narrative arc that shows how the week's events connect and build on one another. Titles must contain only verifiable facts, no opinion or editorialising.

**Body** paragraphs must add context and nuance beyond the headline. Do not use bullet points. Explain any non-obvious acronyms or jargon on first use.

**Contextualising statistics:** Never report a number in isolation. Every quantitative fact must be accompanied by at least one of the following: a comparison to a prior period (e.g. a week, month, or year earlier), a share of a relevant total (e.g. as a percentage of GDP, national capacity, or population), or a ranking relative to peers. A statistic without context is not informative and must be either contextualised or omitted. Do not use emojis.

**Strict factual standard — apply to every sentence:**

- Include only **events that occurred** (votes taken, laws passed, figures released, agreements signed, arrests made, prices recorded) and **objective facts** (statistics, measurements, named parties to a transaction, official outcomes).
- **Exclude entirely:** statements, quotes, promises, warnings, predictions, denials, calls for action, expressions of concern, and any other speech acts — even from officials or institutions. The fact that someone *said* something is not itself a fact about the world.
- If the only available information on a topic is what someone claimed or announced, omit that topic rather than report the claim as fact.
- Do not speculate, editorialize, or characterise intent or motivation.

**Causality standard — apply to every title and sentence:**

- Report events as separate, independent facts. Do not imply that one event caused another unless a direct causal mechanism is part of the established, measurable record.
- Avoid constructions such as "as", "following", "amid", "boosting", "driven by", or "in response to" that suggest one event caused or coincided meaningfully with another. Multiple factors almost always contribute to any outcome, and implying a single cause is a form of editorialising.
- If two events occurred in the same week, report them separately. Do not place them in the same sentence or title in a way that implies a relationship between them.

The digest as a whole should read as a coherent account of the week, not a list of unrelated summaries.
